📝 Synthesis Questions

1. How would you use the concept of opportunity cost to evaluate Germany's hospital overcapacity problem?
Opportunity cost of hospital investment is forgone investment in primary care or prevention. Germany's high bed density (~8.0/1,000 vs. OECD 4.4) means resources are locked into hospital infrastructure that could alternatively improve ambulatory care and reduce unnecessary admissions.
Opportunity cost only applies to individual choices, not system-level decisions
Hospital overcapacity has no opportunity cost because health spending always benefits health
Opportunity cost is the same as the hospital's operating deficit
✓ Correct! Opportunity cost applies at all levels. The resources spent on maintaining excess hospital capacity (€billions/year) have real alternative uses — primary care expansion, prevention programmes, mental health services, digitalisation.
2. Using both AGIL (Parsons) and market failure theory (Arrow), analyse the G-BA's role in the German health system.
From AGIL: G-BA fulfills Goal Attainment (setting benefit catalog), Integration (coordinating payers/providers), and Latency (reproducing corporatist values). From Arrow: G-BA corrects information asymmetry (expert assessment of clinical evidence) and addresses uncertainty (standardised benefit decisions reduce random variation in coverage).
AGIL and Arrow are incompatible frameworks that cannot be applied together
The G-BA has no connection to either AGIL or market failure theory
AGIL only applies to hospitals, not to governance bodies
✓ Correct! Integrating frameworks shows that the G-BA serves both a systemic function (AGIL: integration and goal attainment) and an economic function (correcting market failures through collective decision-making under uncertainty).
3. Apply the "Fixes that Fail" archetype to DRG-based hospital payment in Germany. What does economics add to this analysis?
DRGs were a fix for rising hospital costs (Fix) → hospitals responded by increasing admissions (Unintended consequence) → total spending continued rising (Problem returns). Economics adds: DRGs changed marginal incentives (per-case payment), making volume maximisation rational for hospitals. The fix failed because it did not change the underlying incentive structure — it only changed the form of the incentive.
DRGs were successful and there were no unintended consequences
The "Fixes that Fail" archetype cannot be applied to payment systems
Economics and systems theory cannot be combined for this analysis
✓ Correct! This integration shows why policy design must consider both system dynamics (feedback loops) and incentive structures (economic motivation). The 2024 hospital reform adds capacity payments to break the volume-revenue link — addressing both dimensions simultaneously.
4. Why does Germany not have an explicit QALY threshold like the UK's NICE? Use both systems theory and economics in your answer.
Systems: Germany's corporatist tradition (Latency function in AGIL) and path-dependent institutional structure resist importing foreign HTA frameworks. The G-BA's consensus-based decision-making process is a product of the system's self-reproduction (autopoiesis). Economics: QALY thresholds involve normative value judgments about the monetary value of health — Germany's political culture prefers implicit rationing through negotiation (AMNOG) rather than explicit threshold-based rationing. The efficiency-equity trade-off is resolved differently in Germany's solidarity-based system than in the UK's tax-funded NHS.
Germany simply has not thought of using QALYs
QALYs are illegal under German law
The EU prohibits member states from using QALY thresholds
✓ Correct! This question demonstrates how integrated analysis reveals deeper institutional and cultural factors that pure economic or pure systems analysis would miss.

🎓 Practice Questions

Practice Question 1

Explain Germany's performance paradox. What is it, and what are three explanations?
Show Answer
Model answer: Germany spends ~12.7% GDP on health (among highest in OECD) but life expectancy is below comparable high-spending countries. Three explanations: (1) Curative bias — over-investment in hospital care, underinvestment in prevention/public health. (2) Sectoral fragmentation — poor coordination between ambulatory and inpatient care produces inefficiency. (3) Dual system inequalities — PKV members get faster access, but average population outcomes are lower because GKV members face access barriers. Connect to Donabedian: high spending on structures does not guarantee good outcomes if processes are suboptimal.

Practice Question 2

What is institutional layering? Give three German examples and explain why layering dominates.
Show Answer
Model answer: Layering = adding new rules/institutions on top of old ones without abolishing them. Examples: (1) Morbi-RSA added to correct adverse selection without abolishing dual system. (2) NPK added to address prevention without restructuring GKV/GGD separation. (3) Gesundheitsfonds centralized contribution collection without abolishing fund competition. Layering dominates because: multiple veto players block fundamental reform (Bundesrat, FDP on PKV, Constitutional Court); path dependency makes existing institutions hard to abolish; incremental change is politically easier than replacement. This produces extraordinary complexity.

Model answer: AMNOG (2010) introduced mandatory early benefit assessment for all new drugs: pharma submits dossier → IQWIG assesses benefit vs comparator → G-BA determines degree → price negotiation with GKV-SV. Practice Question 7

What is the joint decision trap? Apply it to hospital reform in Germany.
Show Answer
Model answer: The joint decision trap (Scharpf) occurs when federal decisions require state government consent, producing lowest-common-denominator outcomes. Hospital reform in Germany: hospital planning is a state competence (Art. 84 GG). The federal government cannot restructure hospital planning without Bundesrat approval. State governments protect "their" hospitals for regional economic reasons. Result: KHVVG 2024 achieved modest coordination but could not fundamentally reduce overcapacity. Federalism blocks rationalization. Each state vetoes closure of its own hospitals. The trap is structural — only a critical juncture (e.g., massive financial crisis) could break it.

Practice Question 8

Explain how Power Resources Theory accounts for the creation of the GKV in 1883 and the dual GKV/PKV system in 1955.
Show Answer
Model answer: Power Resources Theory (Korpi) argues welfare states reflect the relative power of labour vs capital. 1883: The rising SPD (socialist movement) threatened Bismarck's conservative order. Repression alone (Anti-Socialist Laws 1878) failed. Bismarck responded with a strategic concession: state social insurance to undercut socialist appeal. The GKV's design (occupational fragmentation, employer involvement) reflected the power balance — workers got benefits but organized in separate funds that prevented cross-industry solidarity. 1955: After WWII, the middle class and professional groups had strong political resources in Adenauer's CDU coalition. They demanded a separate private track to avoid pooling with workers. The SPD could not block it. Result: the dual system, which institutionalizes status differentiation. Both cases show welfare state design = outcome of political power struggles.
