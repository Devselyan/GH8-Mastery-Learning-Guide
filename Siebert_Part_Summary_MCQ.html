Siebert 01: Epidemiology & Social Inequalities

Phase 1: Knowledge Summary

Core Foundations

Epidemiology: The study of the distribution (frequencies/patterns) and determinants (causes/factors) of health-related states in human populations.

Social Epidemiology: A specific branch focusing on how social-structural factors (power, money, resources) affect patterns of disease and health distribution.

Socio-Economic Status (SES): A composite measure of social position based on three primary dimensions:

  • Income: Financial resources.
  • Education: Knowledge and credentials (e.g., CASMIN classification).
  • Occupation: Status of employment (e.g., ISEI index).

Goals of Epidemiology

  • Describe: Prevalence and incidence in populations.
  • Analyze: Course, prognosis, and etiology (determinants).
  • Evaluate: Preventive and therapeutic interventions.
  • Inform: Decision makers and the policy cycle.

Causality Concepts

  • Necessary Cause: Factor must be present for the outcome to occur.
  • Sufficient Cause: A set of factors that inevitably lead to the outcome.
  • Multifactorial: Complex associations where multiple component causes interact.

Hill Guidelines for Causation

  • Strength: Magnitude of association (High HR/RR).
  • Temporality: Exposure precedes outcome (Critical).
  • Consistency: Found across different studies/populations.
  • Dose-Response: Risk increases with higher exposure.
  • Biological Rationale: Plausible biological mechanism.
  • Reversibility: Removing factor reduces risk.

Cross-Sectional Studies

A "Point-in-time" concept examining exposure and outcome simultaneously.

Prevalence (P): Proportion of diseased individuals at a point in time.

P = (a + c) / (a + b + c + d)

Odds Ratio (OR): Cross-product formula for disease chance.

OR = (a * d) / (b * c)

Prevalence Ratio (PR): Ratio of prevalence in exposed vs. non-exposed.

PR = [a / (a + b)] / [c / (c + d)]
