Module 1: Basics — Prevalence vs. Incidence 10 questions
Question 1 Multiple Choice

Which of the following BEST describes prevalence?

Correct Answer: B
Prevalence measures all existing cases (old + new) at one point in time — a "snapshot" of disease burden. Incidence (option A) tracks only new cases over time.
Question 2 Multiple Choice

Which study design is BEST suited for measuring incidence?

Correct Answer: C
Incidence measures new cases over time, which requires following a population forward. Only cohort (and experimental) studies can do this. Cross-sectional studies measure prevalence; case-control studies calculate odds ratios.
Question 3 True / False

Prevalence can increase even if incidence stays the same, because people with the disease are living longer.

Correct Answer: True
If people with a disease live longer (due to better treatment), existing cases accumulate, raising prevalence even if the rate of new cases (incidence) is unchanged. Prevalence and incidence are related — prevalence depends on both how many new cases occur (incidence) and how long people live with the disease (duration).
Question 4 Short Answer

In a town of 10,000 people, 500 have diabetes on January 1st. During the year, 200 new cases are diagnosed. Explain the conceptual difference between prevalence and incidence using this example. Why would the denominator for incidence differ from the denominator for prevalence?

Model Answer

Prevalence is a snapshot — all existing cases at a point in time (the 500 people with diabetes on Jan 1). It describes the total disease burden in the population.

Incidence tracks new cases developing over a period (the 200 newly diagnosed during the year). It measures the rate at which healthy people become ill.

The denominator differs because incidence only concerns the population at risk — people who do NOT already have the disease and could potentially develop it. The 500 people who already have diabetes on Jan 1 cannot develop it again, so they are excluded from the incidence denominator. Prevalence includes the whole population because it counts all cases regardless of when they started.

Question 5 Case Study
Case: Period Poverty in India (Alam et al. 2024)
Researchers used the National Family Health Survey-5 to determine the percentage of women experiencing period poverty at the time of the survey.

Does this study measure prevalence or incidence? Explain why.

Answer

This measures prevalence.

  • The NHFS-5 is a cross-sectional survey — it captures data at one point in time.
  • It asks about current menstruation-related challenges, not new cases developing over time.
  • The result is a proportion of women experiencing period poverty at that moment, which is a prevalence estimate.
  • To measure incidence, researchers would need to follow women over time and track new onset of period poverty.
Question 6 True / False

Prevalence is useful for understanding the burden of chronic diseases in a population, while incidence is more useful for studying disease aetiology.

Correct Answer: True
Prevalence is a snapshot of existing cases — ideal for planning healthcare resources for chronic conditions. Incidence tracks new cases over time — better for identifying risk factors and causes because it captures the rate at which healthy people become ill.
Question 7 Short Answer

A health minister needs to decide how many diabetes clinics to build. Should she look at prevalence data or incidence data? Explain why.

Model Answer
She should look at prevalence — the total number of existing diabetes cases in the population. Prevalence tells her how many people currently need care, which determines the required capacity of clinics. Incidence would tell her how many new cases develop each year, which is useful for forecasting future demand but doesn't capture the existing treatment burden.
Question 8 Short Answer

Why can't cross-sectional studies measure incidence? What study design would you need instead?

Model Answer
Cross-sectional studies measure exposure and outcome at a single point in time. Incidence requires tracking people forward over time to observe when new cases develop, which cross-sectional designs cannot do because they lack a time dimension. To measure incidence, you need a cohort study (or experimental study) that follows a disease-free population forward and records new cases as they occur.
Question 9 Multiple Choice

Which scenario would cause prevalence to increase without a change in incidence?

Correct Answer: B
If people live longer with a disease (better treatment), existing cases accumulate over time, raising prevalence even if the rate of new cases (incidence) stays the same. Options A increases incidence. Options C and D decrease incidence — both would eventually lower prevalence.
Question 10 Multiple Choice

A study recruits 5,000 cancer-free adults and follows them for 15 years to see who develops cancer. Which measure can this study directly estimate?

Correct Answer: B
The study starts with a disease-free cohort and follows them forward, recording new cases over 15 years — this is a cohort study that directly measures cumulative incidence. Prevalence at baseline would be 0 (all were cancer-free by design). Point prevalence at the end would be confounded by deaths and loss to follow-up.
Module 2: Study Designs Exam Focus 10 questions
Question 6 Multiple Choice

A study identifies 200 people who exercise regularly and 200 who do not, then follows both groups for 10 years to see who develops heart disease. What study design is this?

Correct Answer: C
Starting with exposure status (exercisers vs. non-exercisers) and following forward in time to see who develops the outcome = cohort study. The key features are: exposure-based grouping + prospective follow-up.
Question 7 Multiple Choice Exam Focus

Which measure is the PRIMARY or ONLY measure possible in a case-control study?

Correct Answer: C
In case-control studies, you select based on disease status (cases vs. controls), so you cannot calculate incidence or risk directly. The Odds Ratio is the only valid ratio measure. For rare diseases, the OR approximates the RR — but this is an approximation, not a direct calculation.
Question 8 True / False

A cross-sectional study can establish causality because it measures exposure and outcome at the same point in time.

Correct Answer: False
Cross-sectional studies suffer from temporal ambiguity — because exposure and outcome are measured simultaneously, you cannot determine which came first. This is the key limitation that prevents establishing causality.
Question 9 Short Answer Exam Focus

List the three main observational study designs and state one advantage of each.

Model Answer
  1. Cross-sectional: Quick and cheap; good for describing disease burden (prevalence) in a population
  2. Case-control: Efficient for rare diseases; requires fewer participants; faster than cohort studies
  3. Cohort: Best for establishing causality; can measure incidence and multiple outcomes; less prone to recall bias
Question 10 Case Study Exam Focus
Scenario: You want to investigate whether drinking sugary beverages is associated with pancreatic cancer — a relatively rare disease. You have limited funding and need results within 2 years.

Which study design would you choose? Justify your answer.

Model Answer

Best choice: Case-control study

  • Rare disease: Pancreatic cancer is rare, so a cohort study would need to follow a huge population for many years to get enough cases.
  • Limited resources & time: Case-control is cheaper and faster — you identify existing cases and controls, then look back at their beverage consumption.
  • Trade-off: You'll get an Odds Ratio, not a Relative Risk. You'll also need to watch for recall bias (cases may remember their diet differently than controls).
Question 11 Multiple Choice

What is the primary measure of association in a cross-sectional study?

Correct Answer: B
Cross-sectional studies primarily measure prevalence. For association, they calculate a Prevalence Odds Ratio (or Prevalence Ratio). RR, HR, and NNT all require following people forward in time, which cross-sectional designs cannot do.
Question 12 Short Answer Exam Focus

Explain why randomisation is considered the gold standard for establishing causality. What does it achieve that observational designs cannot?

Model Answer
Randomisation balances both known and unknown confounders between treatment and control groups. Observational designs can only adjust for measured confounders — unmeasured or unknown confounders may still bias the results. By creating exchangeable groups, randomisation ensures that any difference in outcome can be attributed to the intervention rather than pre-existing differences. This is why RCTs sit at the top of the evidence hierarchy.
Question 13 Multiple Choice Exam Focus

Which type of bias is of greatest concern in case-control studies when participants are asked about past exposures?

Correct Answer: B
Case-control studies rely on retrospective recall of past exposures. Cases (who already have the disease) may remember exposures differently than healthy controls — they might search their memory more thoroughly or over-report exposures they believe caused their illness. This differential recall between cases and controls is recall bias, a form of information bias.
Question 14 Short Answer

What is the main advantage of a cohort study over a case-control study for studying the causes of a disease? What is one disadvantage?

Model Answer
Advantage: Cohort studies follow people forward in time, so they can measure incidence and calculate Relative Risk directly. They are also less prone to recall bias because exposure information is collected before the outcome develops. They can study multiple outcomes from a single exposure.
Disadvantage: Cohort studies are expensive and time-consuming, especially for rare diseases or diseases with long latency periods. They require large sample sizes and long follow-up, and can suffer from loss to follow-up.
Question 15 True / False

An ecological study uses groups (e.g., countries or regions) as the unit of analysis, which means its findings may not apply to individuals within those groups.

Correct Answer: True
This is the ecological fallacy — associations observed at the group level may not hold at the individual level. For example, a country with high meat consumption may have high cancer rates, but that doesn't mean every individual who eats meat will get cancer. Ecological studies are useful for generating hypotheses but cannot establish individual-level causation.
Module 3: Effect Measures — RR, OR, AR, NNT Exam Focus 10 questions
Question 12 Multiple Choice

A study finds RR = 0.4 for a new vaccine against malaria. How do you interpret this?

Correct Answer: B
RR = 0.4 means the risk in the vaccinated group is 0.4 times (40% of) the risk in the unvaccinated group. This is a protective effect — a 60% reduction (1 − 0.4 = 0.6 = 60%). Option A is wrong because RR < 1 means protection, not increased risk.
Question 13 Multiple Choice Exam Focus

When the disease is common (e.g., 30% prevalence), the Odds Ratio will tend to:

Correct Answer: C
The OR approximates the RR well only when the disease is rare (<10% prevalence). For common outcomes, the OR overestimates the RR. This is because odds are always larger than probabilities when the event is common. This is a frequent exam trap!
Question 14 Short Answer

Explain the conceptual difference between Relative Risk (RR) and Attributable Risk (AR). Why might a public health official care more about AR than RR?

Model Answer
  • RR is a ratio — it tells you how many times more (or less) likely the outcome is in the exposed group. It measures the strength of association but not the absolute burden.
  • AR is a difference — it tells you the absolute excess risk in the exposed group (e.g., "15 extra cases per 1,000 people").
  • Why public health cares about AR: AR tells you the actual impact — how many cases could be prevented if the exposure were removed. An exposure with RR = 20 (very strong) might have a small AR if the disease is rare, while an exposure with RR = 2 might have a large AR if the disease is common.
Question 15 Short Answer Exam Focus

A cohort study finds that people exposed to air pollution have a higher risk of asthma than unexposed people. The RR is 4.0 and the AR (risk difference) is 6%.

Explain the conceptual difference between RR and AR. Why might a public health official care more about the AR than the RR when deciding whether to intervene?

Model Answer
  • RR (Relative Risk) is a ratio — it tells you how many times more likely the outcome is in the exposed group. RR = 4.0 means exposed people have 4× the risk. This measures the strength of association but not the absolute burden.
  • AR (Attributable Risk / Risk Difference) tells you the absolute excess risk — how many extra cases occur in the exposed group. AR = 6% means 6 out of every 100 exposed people develop asthma due to air pollution.
  • Why public health cares about AR: AR tells you the actual impact — how many cases could be prevented if the exposure were removed. An exposure with RR = 20 (very strong) might have a small AR if the disease is rare, while an exposure with RR = 2 might have a large AR if the disease is common. For policy decisions, the number of preventable cases (AR) often matters more than the strength of association (RR).
Question 16 Multiple Choice

A new drug has NNT = 100 over 5 years to prevent one heart attack. Which statement is correct?

Correct Answer: B
NNT is the number of people who need to receive the intervention for one person to benefit. NNT = 100 means you treat 100 people for 5 years and 1 heart attack is prevented. A small NNT means highly effective; a large NNT means modest benefit.
Question 17 Case Study
Case: Diet and Cancer in Spain
A cohort study finds that people who eat a Mediterranean diet have RR = 0.7 for colorectal cancer compared to those who eat a Western diet. The 10-year risk of colorectal cancer in the Western diet group is 3%.

Interpret these findings. Why is it important to consider both the RR and the baseline risk when evaluating the clinical or public health importance of this result?

Model Answer

RR = 0.7 means people on a Mediterranean diet have 0.7 times the risk of those on a Western diet — a 30% relative risk reduction. However, the baseline risk in the Western diet group is 3% over 10 years, which is relatively low. This means the absolute risk reduction is modest.

A 30% relative reduction sounds impressive, but applied to a low baseline risk it translates to a small absolute benefit. Conversely, the same RR = 0.7 applied to a disease with 50% baseline risk would mean a large absolute benefit.

Key point: RR tells you the strength of the association but not the actual impact. For clinical and policy decisions, you need to consider the baseline risk to understand how many people would actually benefit. A large RR with a rare outcome may matter less than a modest RR with a common outcome.

Question 16 Case Study
Scenario: A team of global health experts is investigating a new insecticide-treated net distribution programme in a rural region of Malawi. They compare malaria incidence in villages that received the nets (n = 2,000) vs. villages that did not (n = 2,000). The study reports RR = 0.5.

The minister of health asks your team: "Does this mean the programme works? And how should I explain this to parliament?" Based on the RR alone, what would you tell her — and what else would you want to know before making a funding recommendation?

Model Answer
RR = 0.5 means villages with the nets have half the malaria risk of villages without them — a 50% relative reduction. This suggests the programme is effective. However, before recommending funding, you need: (1) the absolute risk/baseline incidence — a 50% reduction from 2% to 1% is very different from 50% to 25%; (2) the 95% CI — is the estimate precise or could the true effect be much smaller?; (3) the study quality — were villages comparable? Was there adequate blinding of outcome assessment?; (4) cost and feasibility — is the programme scalable and cost-effective compared to alternatives?
Question 17 Case Study Exam Focus
Scenario: A team investigates whether pesticide exposure in field workers is associated with Glioblastoma, a rare brain cancer. They identify 50 patients with Glioblastoma (cases) and 200 healthy controls from the same region, then ask both groups about past pesticide exposure. The team reports an Odds Ratio of 2.8.

At a conference, a colleague argues: "Why didn't you calculate a Relative Risk? That would be more interpretable!" As a member of the research team, how would you respond? Justify why an OR was the correct choice and what the colleague misunderstands about study designs.

Model Answer
In a case-control study, the investigator determines the ratio of cases to controls (here 50:200, but they could have chosen 50:400). Because this sampling ratio is investigator-controlled, the proportion of cases in the study does not reflect the true disease risk in the population. Without knowing the true risk, you cannot calculate a Risk Ratio, which requires risk in exposed ÷ risk in unexposed.
The Odds Ratio is valid because it compares the odds of exposure in cases vs. controls — a comparison that is unaffected by the case-control sampling ratio. The colleague misunderstands that each study design has its own valid effect measure, and case-control studies inherently estimate ORs, not RRs. For rare diseases like Glioblastoma, the OR approximates the RR, but it remains an OR.
Question 18 True / False

Number Needed to Treat (NNT) depends on the baseline risk of the outcome — the same relative risk reduction can produce very different NNT values depending on how common the disease is.

Correct Answer: True
NNT is the inverse of the absolute risk reduction. A treatment that reduces risk by 50% (RR = 0.5) will have a small NNT if the baseline risk is high (many events prevented) but a large NNT if the baseline risk is low (few events prevented). This is why clinicians need to consider baseline risk when applying trial results to their patients — the same treatment may have very different NNTs in different populations.
Question 19 Case Study
Case: Two news articles report on the same medication:
Article A: "New drug reduces heart attack risk by 40%!"
Article B: "New drug prevents 2 heart attacks per 1,000 patients treated."

Both articles are factually correct. Why might they report such different numbers? Which number is more relevant for a patient deciding whether to take the medication?

Model Answer
Article A reports the relative risk reduction (RRR = 40%), which always sounds impressive regardless of baseline risk. Article B reports the absolute risk reduction (ARR = 0.2% or 2 per 1,000), which is modest. Both can be true simultaneously — a 40% relative reduction applied to a low baseline risk yields a small absolute benefit.
For a patient deciding whether to take the medication, the absolute benefit (Article B) is more relevant — it tells them how likely they are to actually benefit. The relative risk reduction can be misleading when the baseline risk is low. This is a common issue in health communication and shared decision-making.
Module 4: Bias, Confounding & Effect Modification Exam Focus 10 questions
Question 18 Multiple Choice

Which of the following is NOT a criterion for a variable to be a confounder?

Correct Answer: C
A confounder must NOT be on the causal pathway. If it is on the pathway (an intermediate variable), adjusting for it would over-adjust and bias the effect estimate. The three criteria are: (1) associated with exposure, (2) independent risk factor for outcome, and (3) not an intermediate variable.
Question 19 Multiple Choice Exam Focus

A study finds that coffee drinkers have a higher risk of lung cancer. After adjusting for smoking, the effect disappears. This is an example of:

Correct Answer: B
Smoking is associated with coffee drinking (coffee drinkers smoke more) AND smoking causes lung cancer. When you adjust for smoking, the coffee-lung cancer association disappears — meaning the original association was confounded by smoking. This is the classic confounding example.
Question 20 True / False

Effect modification and confounding are the same thing — both distort the exposure-outcome relationship.

Correct Answer: False
Confounding distorts the association — you want to remove it.
Effect modification (interaction) means the effect differs across strata — you want to report it, not remove it.
Question 21 Short Answer Exam Focus

List three strategies for managing confounders at the design stage and three at the analysis stage.

Model Answer

Design stage (before data collection):

  1. Randomization — balances known and unknown confounders (gold standard, but only in experimental studies)
  2. Restriction — limit study to one level of the confounder (e.g., only non-smokers)
  3. Matching — select controls with same confounder distribution as cases (common in case-control studies)

Analysis stage (after data collection):

  1. Stratification — split data into confounder levels and compare effect estimates
  2. Multivariable regression — include confounders as covariates to get an "adjusted" effect
  3. Standardization — apply standard population weights to remove structural differences
Question 22 Case Study
Case: Malaria in Mozambique
A study finds that children living near swamps have higher malaria rates. However, families near swamps also have lower incomes, and lower income is associated with less access to bed nets and antimalarial treatment.

Identify the potential confounder(s). How might you control for them?

Model Answer

Potential confounders: Income / socioeconomic status (SES)

  • Income is associated with exposure (living near swamps — poorer families live in less desirable areas)
  • Income is an independent risk factor for outcome (lower income → less prevention/treatment → more malaria)
  • Income is not on the causal pathway between swamp proximity and malaria

Control strategies:

  • Design: Match children from similar income levels; restrict to one income group
  • Analysis: Stratify by income; include income in a multivariable regression model to get an adjusted effect of swamp proximity
Question 23 Multiple Choice Exam Focus

A study finds that people who drink red wine live longer than non-drinkers. However, red wine drinkers tend to have higher incomes and better education. Which type of bias is this?

Correct Answer: B
Socioeconomic status (income, education) is associated with both the exposure (red wine drinking) and the outcome (longer life), but is not on the causal pathway. This is the classic confounding pattern — the apparent wine-longevity association may be partly or entirely due to the confounder. Adjusting for SES in the analysis would give a less biased estimate of wine's true effect.
Question 24 Multiple Choice

Which study design is least susceptible to selection bias?

Correct Answer: B
A cohort study with high follow-up starts with a defined population and tracks them forward, minimising selection bias because exposure is measured before the outcome and the investigator does not select participants based on disease status. Case-control studies are especially vulnerable to selection bias because the choice of controls can introduce systematic differences. Cross-sectional studies can suffer from non-response bias.
Question 25 Case Study Exam Focus
Scenario: A study in rural India finds that children who participate in a school-based nutrition programme have lower rates of anaemia (RR = 0.6). However, participating schools have better water sanitation infrastructure than non-participating schools. Separate analyses show that the nutrition programme effect is RR = 0.55 in schools with good sanitation and RR = 0.58 in schools with poor sanitation — nearly identical. Meanwhile, children in participating schools also tend to come from higher-income families, which independently predicts lower anaemia.

Which of these two factors — water sanitation and family income — represents confounding and which might be effect modification? How would you handle each in your analysis? Justify.

Model Answer
Family income is a confounder: It is associated with programme participation (higher-income families more likely to enrol) AND independently predicts anaemia — but the RR does not differ across income strata. You want to adjust for it (e.g., include income in a regression model) to isolate the programme's independent effect.
Water sanitation is NOT a confounder or effect modifier: The effect is nearly identical across sanitation levels (RR ≈ 0.55–0.58), meaning sanitation does not modify the effect. However, if participating schools systematically have better sanitation, and sanitation independently affects anaemia, it could still be a confounder and should be adjusted for.
Key distinction: Confounding distorts the estimate (adjust for it). Effect modification means the effect truly differs across groups (report it — don't adjust it away). Here, the nearly identical RRs suggest no effect modification by sanitation. The practical step: include both income and sanitation in a multivariable model to get the adjusted programme effect.
Question 26 True / False

If a confounder is measured in a study, it can be controlled for during the analysis stage using multivariable regression.

Correct Answer: True
If a confounder is measured accurately, it can be included as a covariate in a regression model to obtain an adjusted effect estimate. This is one of the main reasons multivariable regression is widely used in epidemiology. However, unmeasured or poorly measured confounders cannot be controlled for in analysis, which is why randomisation at the design stage is the gold standard — it balances both measured and unmeasured confounders.
Question 27 Short Answer

What is selection bias and when is it most likely to occur in epidemiological studies? Give an example.

Model Answer
Selection bias occurs when the association between exposure and outcome differs between those who participate in the study and those who would have been eligible but did not participate. It is most likely when participation is related to both exposure and outcome.
Example: A case-control study of a rare disease recruits controls from a hospital. If the disease is related to lifestyle factors and the hospital controls are also more health-conscious than the general population, the exposure distribution in controls may be biased, distorting the OR. This is why control selection is critical in case-control studies.
Module 5: Statistical Concepts 10 questions
Question 23 Multiple Choice

A researcher measures income in categories: <€20k, €20k–€40k, €40k–€60k, >€60k. What type of variable is this?

Correct Answer: C
The categories have a natural order (low → high), so this is ordinal, not nominal (unordered categories). If actual income in euros were recorded, it would be continuous numerical. The grouping into ordered categories makes it ordinal.
Question 24 Multiple Choice

You have data on hospital stay duration (in days) that is right-skewed with several extreme outliers. Which measure of central tendency and dispersion should you report?

Correct Answer: B
For skewed data with outliers, the mean is pulled in the direction of the skew and SD becomes misleading. The median (resistant to outliers) with IQR is the correct choice. Mean ± SD is appropriate only for approximately normally distributed data.
Question 25 Short Answer

A study reports that "the mean age of participants was 45 years (SD = 12)" and the data is approximately normally distributed. Explain what the standard deviation tells you about the age distribution. Why is the mean ± SD more informative than reporting the mean alone?

Model Answer

The standard deviation (SD) measures the spread or dispersion of the data around the mean. SD = 12 means that ages typically vary by about 12 years above or below the average of 45.

Without the SD, you only know the central tendency (45 years) but have no idea whether participants ranged from 30 to 60 or from 20 to 80. The SD gives you a sense of the variability — a larger SD means more heterogeneous participants.

The mean alone can be misleading: two studies could both report mean age = 45, but one with SD = 3 (very homogeneous, mostly 42–48) and another with SD = 15 (very spread out, 30–60 range). Reporting both mean and SD gives a much more complete picture of the sample.

Question 28 Multiple Choice

Which type of variable is blood type (A, B, AB, O)?

Correct Answer: C
Blood types have no natural order (A is not "greater than" B, etc.) — they are categories without ranking. This makes them nominal categorical variables. Ordinal variables (like cancer stage I–IV) do have a meaningful order. Numerical variables are measured on a numeric scale.
Question 29 True / False

For a right-skewed distribution, the mean is typically greater than the median.

Correct Answer: True
In a right-skewed distribution, the tail extends to the right (higher values). The mean is pulled in the direction of the skew, making it larger than the median. The median, being resistant to extreme values, remains closer to the centre. This is why median is preferred for skewed data — it better represents the "typical" value.
Question 30 Short Answer

Why is it misleading to report only the mean without a measure of dispersion? What could go wrong in interpretation?

Model Answer
The mean alone tells you the central tendency but nothing about the spread or variability of the data. Two datasets can have identical means but very different distributions — one tightly clustered around the mean, another widely spread out. For example, two hospitals could both report mean patient satisfaction of 7/10, but one has all scores between 6-8 while the other has scores ranging from 1-10. Without a measure of dispersion (SD, IQR, range), you cannot assess the homogeneity of the data or the reliability of the mean.
Question 31 Case Study
Case: A study on hospital stay duration reports mean = 8 days, median = 5 days. The researchers used mean ± SD to describe their data.

What does the difference between mean and median tell you about the distribution? Was the choice of mean ± SD appropriate? What would you recommend instead?

Model Answer
The mean (8) being higher than the median (5) indicates right-skew — a minority of patients with very long stays are pulling the mean upward. Using mean ± SD is not appropriate for skewed data because the SD interpretation (symmetric spread around the mean) is misleading when the distribution is asymmetric. Recommend: report median ± IQR (or median and range). The median of 5 days better represents the typical patient, and the IQR shows the spread of the middle 50% of patients.
Question 32 Multiple Choice

When would you use the mode as the preferred measure of central tendency?

Correct Answer: B
The mode is the most frequently occurring value in a dataset. It is most useful for categorical data (e.g., "what is the most common blood type?") where mean and median cannot be meaningfully calculated. For continuous data, the mode is less useful because there may be many unique values.
Question 33 Short Answer

If a variable is measured on an ordinal scale (e.g., pain level: none, mild, moderate, severe), which measures of central tendency and dispersion are appropriate?

Model Answer
For ordinal data, the median and IQR (or frequency distributions) are appropriate. The mean and SD are not appropriate because they assume equal intervals between categories, which ordinal data does not guarantee (the difference between "none" and "mild" may not equal the difference between "moderate" and "severe"). The median identifies the middle category, and the IQR shows the spread of the middle 50%. Alternatively, presenting the proportion in each category is informative.
Question 34 True / False

A histogram can reveal the shape of a distribution — including whether it is symmetric, skewed, or has multiple peaks.

Correct Answer: True
A histogram is a key graphical tool for understanding the distribution of a continuous variable. It shows the overall shape (symmetric, left-skewed, right-skewed), central tendency, spread, and whether there are multiple modes (peaks). Visualising data with a histogram should always be one of the first steps in data analysis — before calculating any summary statistics.
Module 6: Confidence Intervals & P-values 10 questions
Question 26 Multiple Choice

What is the correct interpretation of a 95% confidence interval?

Correct Answer: B
The CI is a property of the procedure, not the specific interval. Option A is the common misinterpretation (Bayesian interpretation, not frequentist). The correct frequentist interpretation is: under repeated sampling, 95% of such intervals will contain the true value.
Question 27 Multiple Choice

A study reports: OR = 2.0, 95% CI: 0.9 to 4.5. Is this result statistically significant?

Correct Answer: B
For ratio measures (OR, RR, HR), statistical significance at α = 0.05 is determined by whether the 95% CI includes the null value (1). Since the CI (0.9 to 4.5) includes 1, we cannot reject the null hypothesis — the result is NOT statistically significant, even though the point estimate (OR = 2.0) suggests increased risk.
Question 28 True / False

A p-value < 0.05 means there is a 95% probability that the research hypothesis is true.

Correct Answer: False
This is a common misconception. A p-value is the probability of observing the data (or more extreme) assuming the null hypothesis is true. It does NOT tell you the probability that either hypothesis is true. p < 0.05 means there is less than a 5% chance of seeing such extreme data if the null were true.
Question 29 Short Answer

A trial of a new hypertension drug reports a statistically significant reduction in blood pressure (p = 0.003) but the effect size is only 2 mmHg. Is this finding clinically important? Explain.

Model Answer

Statistical significance ≠ clinical importance.

  • A large sample size can make even a tiny effect (2 mmHg) statistically significant (p = 0.003).
  • Whether 2 mmHg reduction is clinically important depends on context: for population-level prevention, even small reductions can be meaningful; for an individual patient, 2 mmHg may not justify medication side effects.
  • Always examine both the effect size and confidence interval, not just the p-value.
Question 35 Multiple Choice

A 95% confidence interval for an odds ratio is 1.5 to 3.2. What can you conclude?

Correct Answer: C
Since the entire 95% CI (1.5 to 3.2) is above 1, the result is statistically significant (the null value is excluded). However, the interval ranges from 1.5 to 3.2 — from a modest to a strong effect — indicating imprecision in the estimate. A narrow CI would indicate more precision. This illustrates why CIs are more informative than p-values alone: they tell you both statistical significance and the range of plausible effect sizes.
Question 36 Short Answer Exam Focus

What factors influence the width of a confidence interval? Why is a narrow CI generally preferred?

Model Answer
Factors affecting CI width: (1) Sample size — larger samples produce narrower CIs (more precision); (2) Variability in the data — less variability produces narrower CIs; (3) Confidence level — higher confidence (e.g., 99%) produces wider CIs than lower confidence (e.g., 90%).
A narrow CI is preferred because it indicates greater precision — the estimate is more reliable and the range of plausible true values is smaller. A wide CI means the study results are compatible with a broad range of possible effects, making the estimate less useful for decision-making.
Question 37 Case Study Exam Focus
Case: A large RCT (n = 50,000) finds that a new drug reduces cardiovascular events with RR = 0.95, p = 0.01. A smaller RCT (n = 500) of the same drug finds RR = 0.60, p = 0.10.

Which result is more clinically meaningful? Why might the p-values differ?

Model Answer
The large trial (RR = 0.95, p = 0.01) found a statistically significant but clinically modest effect — only a 5% relative risk reduction. It achieved significance mainly because the massive sample size gave it high power to detect even tiny effects.
The small trial (RR = 0.60, p = 0.10) found a clinically large effect (40% reduction) but was underpowered — the result was not statistically significant because the sample was too small.
Key lesson: Statistical significance does not equal clinical importance. Always examine the effect size and CI, not just the p-value. The large trial's tiny effect may be real but trivial; the small trial's large effect may be important but uncertain.
Question 38 True / False

A Type I error (false positive) occurs when you reject the null hypothesis when it is actually true. The significance level (α) is the probability of making a Type I error.

Correct Answer: True
Type I error (α): Concluding there is an effect when there really isn't — false positive. The significance level α (conventionally 0.05) is the probability of this error.
Type II error (β): Concluding there is no effect when there really is — false negative. Power = 1 − β is the probability of correctly detecting a true effect. Researchers need to balance both types of error, which is reflected in sample size planning.
Question 39 Short Answer

Why is it problematic to rely solely on p-values for scientific decision-making? What additional information should researchers always report?

Model Answer
P-values have several limitations: (1) They do not tell you about the size or importance of an effect — a tiny, clinically meaningless effect can be statistically significant with a large enough sample. (2) A non-significant p-value does not prove the null hypothesis — the study may be underpowered. (3) P-values are frequently misinterpreted as the probability that the null hypothesis is true. (4) They provide a binary (significant/not) that ignores the continuous nature of evidence.
Researchers should always report: effect size (e.g., RR, OR, mean difference) and confidence interval alongside the p-value. The CI provides information about precision and the range of plausible effects, which is much more informative.
Question 40 Multiple Choice

When multiple statistical tests are performed on the same data, the risk of at least one Type I error:

Correct Answer: B
With α = 0.05 for each test, performing 20 independent tests means the probability of at least one false positive is about 1 − (0.95)^20 ≈ 64%. This is the multiple testing problem. Corrections exist (Bonferroni, Benjamini-Hochberg) but they reduce power. This issue is especially important in subgroup analyses, where false positives can lead to incorrect conclusions about which patient groups benefit from treatment.

Explain what it means to "adjust for confounders" in a regression model. Why might the adjusted effect estimate differ from the crude (unadjusted) estimate?

Model Answer
  • Adjusting means including confounder variables as additional predictors in the regression model alongside the exposure.
  • The model then estimates the effect of the exposure "holding confounders constant" — i.e., comparing people with the same confounder values but different exposure levels.

In a stepped-wedge design, all clusters start in control. At regular intervals, a randomly selected subset crosses over to intervention. By the end, all clusters have received the intervention. This is ethically appealing when you cannot withhold an effective intervention.

Question 38 True / False

Correct Answer: True

Question 47 Short Answer Exam Focus

When would a researcher choose a cluster RCT over an individual RCT? Give at least two reasons.

Model Answer
Reasons to choose a cRCT:
(1) Risk of contamination — when the intervention is delivered at the group level (e.g., community health education, water sanitation) and individual randomisation would lead to control participants being exposed to the intervention.
(2) Logistical or ethical reasons — when randomising individuals within the same community or clinic is impractical or could create resentment (e.g., giving different insecticide-treated nets to neighbours in the same village).
(3) Intervention naturally operates at cluster level — e.g., policy changes, school-based programmes, or healthcare system interventions where the unit of delivery is the institution, not the individual.

Question 49 Short Answer

Explain what a stepped-wedge cRCT is and when it is particularly useful.

Model Answer
In a stepped-wedge cRCT, all clusters start in the control condition. At randomly assigned time points, clusters cross over to the intervention, one by one or in groups. By the end of the study, all clusters have received the intervention.

Question 50 True / False

In a pair-matched cRCT, clusters are matched on key characteristics before randomisation to improve balance between arms.

Correct Answer: True
In pair-matched (stratified) cRCTs, clusters are paired based on similar characteristics (e.g., size, baseline outcomes, socioeconomic profile). Within each pair, one cluster is randomised to intervention and one to control. This improves baseline comparability and reduces between-cluster variability, which increases statistical efficiency. However, the analysis must account for the matching (e.g., using paired analysis), and the degrees of freedom are based on the number of pairs, not clusters.
Question 51 Multiple Choice

In the analysis of a cRCT, what is the primary concern if clustering is ignored?

Correct Answer: B
Ignoring clustering treats all individuals as independent observations when they are actually correlated. This underestimates the standard errors, producing p-values that are too small (more false positives) and CIs that are too narrow (overly precise). The effect estimate (e.g., RR) may still be correct, but the precision is overestimated. This is why cRCTs must always use appropriate analytical methods (multi-level models, GEE, or cluster-robust standard errors).
Module 10: ICC & Design Effect 10 questions
Question 39 Multiple Choice

The Intra-Cluster Correlation Coefficient (ICC) ranges from 0 to 1. What does ICC = 0 mean?

Correct Answer: B
ICC = 0 means all variability is within clusters — individuals are independent of their cluster membership. No clustering adjustment is needed. The effective sample size equals the total participants. ICC = 0 is ideal but rarely occurs in cluster-based studies because people within the same cluster tend to be similar.
Question 40 Multiple Choice

The Design Effect (DE) tells you:

Correct Answer: B
The Design Effect quantifies how much larger your sample needs to be due to clustering. The DE depends on the cluster size and the ICC — larger clusters and higher ICC mean more inflation. For example, DE = 2 means you need twice as many participants as an individually randomized trial to maintain the same statistical power.
Question 41 Short Answer

A cRCT has 20 clusters with 30 individuals each (total 600 participants). The ICC is estimated at 0.03.

Explain conceptually why the effective sample size is less than the total number of participants in a cRCT. What factors influence how much information is lost due to clustering?

Model Answer
  • Why effective sample size is smaller: Individuals within a cluster (e.g., same village, school, clinic) are more similar to each other than to people in other clusters — they share environments, resources, and exposures. This similarity means each additional person in the same cluster adds less new information than a person from a different cluster. Statistically, the "independent observations" are fewer than the total participant count.
  • Factors that increase information loss (larger Design Effect):
    • Higher ICC: More similarity within clusters means less independent information per participant.
    • Larger cluster size: Beyond a certain point, adding more participants to an existing cluster yields diminishing new information.
  • Implication: When designing a cRCT, you need to enrol more participants than an individual RCT to achieve the same statistical power. This is why cRCTs are typically larger and more expensive.

  • The Design Effect (DE) quantifies the inflation needed — larger clusters and higher ICC mean greater loss of power.
Question 52 Short Answer

What does the Intra-Cluster Correlation Coefficient (ICC) measure? What does a high ICC tell you about the data?

Model Answer
The ICC measures the degree of similarity of outcomes within the same cluster — how much individuals in the same group resemble each other compared to individuals in different groups. It ranges from 0 to 1.
A high ICC (close to 1) means outcomes are very similar within clusters — most of the variability is between clusters, not within them. This implies that adding more participants to an existing cluster adds little new information, making the Design Effect larger. Question 53 Multiple Choice

Why does clustering reduce statistical power in cRCTs?

Correct Answer: B
Individuals within a cluster share environments, resources, and exposures, making their outcomes more similar than individuals from different clusters. This correlation means each new participant in the same cluster adds less new statistical information than an independent observation. The effective sample size — the number of independent units of information — is therefore smaller than the total number of participants. Question 54 Short Answer

When designing a cRCT, is it better to increase the number of clusters or the size of each cluster to improve statistical power? Explain.

Model Answer
It is almost always better to increase the number of clusters rather than the size of each cluster. Adding more clusters adds independent units of observation, which directly increases power. Adding more participants to existing clusters yields diminishing returns because those individuals are correlated with existing cluster members — beyond a certain cluster size, the information gained per additional participant is very small.
In practice, however, adding clusters is often more expensive (more training sites, more logistics, more communities to engage). The researcher must find the optimal balance: enough clusters to achieve adequate power while keeping cluster sizes reasonable. This trade-off is an important part of cRCT planning.

Module 11: Survival Analysis Exam Focus 10 questions
Question 43 Multiple Choice

What is right-censoring in survival analysis?

Correct Answer: B
Right-censoring occurs when we know a participant survived up to time t but do not observe the event — because the study ended, they withdrew, or were lost to follow-up. Crucially, censored observations are still informative and are included in the analysis up to their censoring time.
Question 44 Multiple Choice

What happens to the Kaplan-Meier survival estimate when a patient is censored at time t?

Correct Answer: B
Censoring does not cause a drop in the survival function. The censored individual is included in the risk set up until their censoring time, then removed from subsequent calculations. The survival probability stays flat — the step only drops when an event (death) occurs.
Question 45 Short Answer

What does the log-rank test compare? What is its null hypothesis?

Model Answer
  • The log-rank test compares the survival distributions (the entire survival curves) of two or more groups.
  • Null hypothesis: The survival functions are equal across groups — S(t)₁ = S(t)₂ (there is no difference in survival between groups).
  • It is a non-parametric test (does not assume a specific survival distribution).
  • It works by comparing observed vs. expected events at each time point across groups.
  • If p < 0.05, we reject the null and conclude the survival curves are significantly different.
Question 46 Case Study Exam Focus
Case: A study follows 200 patients with a rare cancer. 120 receive a new treatment, 80 receive standard care. After 5 years:

- Control group: 60 deaths (20 censored)
A Kaplan-Meier curve is plotted and the log-rank test gives p = 0.002.

What can you conclude? Interpret the survival curves and the p-value together.

Model Answer
  • The log-rank test (p = 0.002) tells us there is a statistically significant difference between the two survival curves.
  • The survival curves would show that the treatment group has a higher probability of surviving at each time point (the treatment curve stays above the control curve).
  • The median survival time would likely be longer in the treatment group.
  • Censoring pattern note: More censoring in the treatment group (60 vs. 20) could mean treatment patients were more likely to be alive at study end — which is consistent with better outcomes.
  • Conclusion: The new treatment is associated with significantly better survival compared to standard care in this patient population.
Question 58 Short Answer Exam Focus

What types of research questions require survival analysis rather than standard regression? Give two examples.

Model Answer
Survival analysis is needed when the outcome is time until an event occurs, especially when censoring is present — i.e., not all participants experience the event during the study period. Standard regression cannot handle censored observations appropriately.
Examples:
(1) Clinical trial: Time from treatment initiation until death or disease recurrence. Patients who are still alive at study end are censored.
(2) Public health: Time from HIV diagnosis until development of AIDS. Patients lost to follow-up are censored at their last visit.
In both cases, survival analysis uses information from censored individuals up to their censoring time, which standard regression would waste.
Question 59 Multiple Choice Exam Focus

A Hazard Ratio (HR) of 0.6 from a Cox regression model means:

Correct Answer: B
A Hazard Ratio compares the instantaneous risk (hazard) of the event between two groups at any point in time. HR = 0.6 means the treated group has 40% lower hazard of the event (e.g., death) compared to controls, assuming proportional hazards. It does NOT directly tell you about median survival (A) or the proportion surviving (C). The HR is the survival analysis equivalent of the RR — it measures the relative rate of events over time.
Question 60 Short Answer

What is the Kaplan-Meier estimator and how would you interpret a Kaplan-Meier survival curve?

Model Answer
The Kaplan-Meier estimator is a non-parametric method for estimating the survival function from time-to-event data. It calculates the probability of surviving past each time point, accounting for censoring. The survival curve is a step function that drops at each event time and stays flat between events.
Interpreting a KM curve: The y-axis shows the survival probability (from 1.0 downwards), the x-axis shows time. A curve that stays higher and declines more slowly indicates better survival. The median survival time (where S(t) = 0.5) can be read off the graph. The log-rank test compares whether two or more KM curves are statistically different. KM curves should always include a table of the number at risk at each time point to show how much data supports each segment.
Question 61 True / False

In survival analysis, censored observations are excluded from the analysis entirely because their outcome is unknown.

Correct Answer: False
Censored observations are not excluded — they contribute valuable information. A censored individual is included in the risk set and contributes to the survival estimate up until their censoring time. They are simply removed from subsequent risk sets. This is one of the key advantages of survival analysis: it uses all available information from every participant, even those who don't experience the event. Excluding censored participants would bias the results by only analysing those who had the event.
Question 62 Multiple Choice

The proportional hazards assumption of Cox regression requires that:

Correct Answer: A
The proportional hazards assumption states that the hazard ratio is constant over time — i.e., the relative risk of the event between groups does not change during follow-up. This does not require the baseline hazards to be the same (they can differ). If survival curves cross, the PH assumption is violated because the HR changes over time. PH should be tested (e.g., using Schoenfeld residuals) before interpreting Cox model results.

Question 64 Short Answer Exam Focus

What are the four main components of a sample size calculation for a study comparing two groups?

Model Answer
The four components are:
(1) Significance level (α) — typically 0.05, the acceptable risk of a Type I error (false positive).
(2) Power (1 − β) — typically 80%, the probability of detecting a true effect if it exists. Higher power needs larger samples.
(3) Effect size — the expected magnitude of the difference between groups. A smaller effect requires a larger sample to detect.
(4) Variability — the expected variation in the outcome (e.g., SD for continuous outcomes, baseline risk for binary outcomes). More variability requires larger samples.
All four must be specified before the study begins. Sample size planning ensures the study is neither underpowered (wasteful, unethical) nor excessively large (wasteful).

If a researcher wants to detect a smaller effect size with the same power and α, the required sample size will:

Correct Answer: B
Detecting a smaller effect requires a larger sample size — the difference you want to detect is more subtle, so you need more participants to distinguish it from random variation. This is intuitive: a very large effect (e.g., a treatment that cures 90% of patients) can be detected with a small study, while a small effect (e.g., 5% risk reduction) requires a much larger study to achieve the same statistical power.

Question 67 Short Answer

Why is it unethical to conduct a study that is too small (underpowered) OR too large? Explain both sides.

Model Answer
Underpowered study (too small): Unethical because participants are exposed to the risks and burdens of research (invasive tests, time, inconvenience, potential side effects) without a reasonable chance of producing meaningful results. The study may fail to detect a real effect, wasting resources and potentially leading to abandonment of a useful intervention. It also violates the principle that research should contribute to generalisable knowledge.
Overpowered study (too large): Unethical because more participants than necessary are exposed to potential harms and burdens. Large studies can detect trivially small effects that are statistically significant but clinically meaningless, potentially leading to adoption of ineffective or harmful interventions based on statistical significance alone. Oversized studies also waste limited research resources that could be used for other studies.
Question 68 Multiple Choice

In a cRCT, the required sample size must be inflated compared to an individual RCT. This inflation is primarily due to:

Correct Answer: B
The sample size inflation in cRCTs is due to the Design Effect (DE), which accounts for the loss of statistical information caused by within-cluster correlation. Individuals in the same cluster are more similar than individuals in different clusters, meaning each additional participant provides less than one unit of independent information. The DE depends on the ICC and the average cluster size. The inflation is not primarily about dropout rates, number of clusters, or confounding — it is fundamentally about information loss due to clustering.
Question 69 Case Study Exam Focus
Case: A researcher submits a grant proposal for a study with 30 participants per arm to detect a 50% reduction in disease risk. The reviewer notes the study is underpowered and the necessary sample size would be 200 per arm.

Why might the researcher have underestimated the required sample size? What are the consequences of proceeding with the smaller study?

Model Answer
The researcher may have underestimated the required sample size by: (1) overestimating the expected effect size (50% reduction may be too optimistic; the true effect may be smaller); (2) underestimating the variability in the outcome; (3) using too low power; or (4) ignoring the Design Effect if it is a cRCT.
Consequences of proceeding with 30 per arm: The study will almost certainly be underpowered — unable to detect a statistically significant effect even if the intervention truly works. The result will likely be "non-significant" (inconclusive), wasting the resources invested and potentially causing a useful intervention to be abandoned. Ethically, participants were exposed to research burdens without a reasonable chance of producing meaningful knowledge. Proper sample size planning in advance is essential to avoid these problems.

Question 70 — Mock Exam Style Case Study
As a project member of a team of experts you are supposed to report existing registry data on incidence of dengue fever infections in four rural regions (A, B, C, and D) at the next team meeting. Since resources are scarce, only one of the four regions will receive funding for improving health care infrastructure. (14 points)

(a) Which commonly used strategies would be helpful to investigate which of the four regions has the highest disease burden and should therefore be selected for implementing the preventive intervention? (2 points)

(b) What is the most important reason for applying one of the strategies from question (a) for the purpose of selecting the region to implement the preventive intervention? (4 points)

(c) Describe the two different approaches of the commonly used strategy from task (a) to identify priority settings for an intervention (no formulas needed). (4 points)

(d) What is the problem when interpreting the numbers resulting from using one of the two approaches on the dengue incidences in the four aforementioned rural regions? (4 points)

Model Answer

(a) Standardisation (age-standardisation or direct/indirect standardisation) of the incidence rates. Also: calculating comparable incidence rates per 1,000 or 100,000 population. Crude rates alone may be misleading if the regions have different age structures.

(b) The most important reason: the four regions likely have different age structures (e.g., Region A may have a younger population, Region D an older one). Since dengue incidence varies by age, comparing crude rates would confound age structure differences with true disease burden differences. Standardisation removes the effect of age differences, allowing a fair comparison of disease burden across regions.

(c) Direct standardisation: Apply region-specific age-specific rates to a standard population (e.g., the WHO world standard population) to calculate what the rate would be if all regions had the same age structure. Indirect standardisation: Apply standard (reference) age-specific rates to each region's population to calculate expected cases, then compare observed to expected (Standardised Incidence Ratio, SIR). Direct is used when region-specific age-specific rates are available; indirect when only the total number of cases and the population age distribution are known.

(d) The choice of standard population affects the resulting standardised rates. Different standard populations (e.g., world standard vs. European standard) can produce different absolute rates and even change the ranking of regions. The standardised rates are relative — they only have meaning in comparison with each other using the same standard. Additionally, standardisation removes real age differences, which may themselves be relevant for public health planning (a region with many young people may need different interventions).

Question 71 — Mock Exam Style Case Study
We can use a variety of strategies to statistically describe distribution and dynamics of disease as well as exposure and outcome relationships or intervention effects in a population. (12 points)

(1) Report which of the different study types we discussed in class would be appropriate for the following four scenarios. (8 points)

(2) Also, report which statistical measure(s) of an exposure/outcome relationship or intervention effect you could use in the different scenarios. (4 points)

Justify your answers to (1) and (2)!

Scenario 1: A team of researchers is interested in investigating factors associated with newly occurring diarrhoea episodes in children under 5 years of age in a rural population of a low-income country. Their focus is on sanitation.

Scenario 2: A team of global health experts is supposed to investigate the association between pesticide exposure in field workers and the occurrence of Glioblastoma, a rare neoplasm of the brain.

Scenario 3: The health authorities in the capital city of a low-income country are interested in investigating the distribution of cardiovascular diseases, and the factors that explain differences in the distribution of those diseases in the general population.

Scenario 4: A team of global health experts is going to investigate the impact of a newly developed system of solar water disinfection compared to the standard system for a rural area with insufficient access to safe drinking water on the frequency of E. coli associated diseases.

Model Answer

Scenario 1 — Cohort study: The research question is about newly occurring diarrhoea (incidence) and its association with sanitation. A cohort study follows disease-free children forward, tracking who develops diarrhoea based on sanitation level. Effect measure: Relative Risk (RR) or Risk Difference (AR). The cohort design allows direct calculation of incidence and risk ratios.

Scenario 2 — Case-control study: Glioblastoma is a rare disease. A cohort study would need an enormous sample followed for many years to obtain enough cases. Selecting cases (with Glioblastoma) and controls (without) and looking back at pesticide exposure is efficient. Effect measure: Odds Ratio (OR). Case-control studies cannot measure incidence, so RR is not directly calculable. For rare diseases, OR approximates RR.

Scenario 3 — Cross-sectional study: The aim is to investigate the distribution of CVD and associated factors in the general population at one point in time. No follow-up is needed. Effect measure: Prevalence Odds Ratio (POR) or Prevalence Ratio (PR). Cross-sectional studies measure prevalence, not incidence, so effect measures are based on prevalence comparisons.

Scenario 4 — Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT) / cluster RCT: The research question is about the impact (effectiveness) of a new intervention. Solar water disinfection is naturally delivered at the community level, so a cluster RCT randomising villages to new vs. standard system would be appropriate to avoid contamination. Effect measure: Relative Risk (RR) or Risk Difference (AR), possibly NNT if reporting in absolute terms. For a cRCT, analysis must account for clustering (ICC, design effect).

Question 72 — Mock Exam Style Case Study
Your team is evaluating a community health worker (CHW) programme aimed at reducing maternal mortality in three districts of rural Zambia (Districts X, Y, Z). District X has been running the programme for 5 years, District Y for 2 years, and District Z has not yet implemented it. Your team has access to registry data on maternal deaths and population demographics. (12 points)

(a) What is the main challenge when comparing maternal mortality across these three districts? (3 points)

(b) Which epidemiological strategy would you use to address this challenge? Describe the two approaches (no formulas needed). (5 points)

(c) After applying the strategy, you find that District X has the lowest rate, followed by Y, then Z. The Minister claims this proves the CHW programme works. Is this conclusion justified? What alternative explanations must be considered? (4 points)

Model Answer

(a) The main challenge is that the three districts likely have different population structures (age distribution, fertility rates, socioeconomic composition). Maternal mortality is strongly age-dependent (higher risk in very young and older mothers). Comparing crude maternal mortality rates would confound population differences with programme effects. Additionally, the districts may differ in completeness of death registration.

(b) Standardisation: (1) Direct standardisation: Apply each district's age-specific maternal mortality rates to a standard population (e.g., Zambia national population or WHO standard) to calculate what the rate would be if all districts had the same age structure. (2) Indirect standardisation: Apply standard (national) age-specific rates to each district's population to calculate expected deaths, then compute the Standardised Mortality Ratio (SMR = observed/expected). Direct is preferable when district-specific age-stratified data are available; indirect when only total deaths and population age distribution are known.

(c) The Minister's conclusion is not fully justified. Alternative explanations: (1) Confounding by secular trends — maternal mortality may have been declining nationally over the 5-year period independent of the programme. (2) Selection bias — District X may have been chosen to pilot the programme because it already had stronger health infrastructure or higher literacy rates. (3) Confounding by other interventions — District X may have also received other health investments during the same period. (4) Differences in reporting — better death registration in some districts could bias comparisons. A more rigorous evaluation would require a controlled design (e.g., difference-in-differences, or a stepped-wedge cRCT).

Question 73 — Mock Exam Style Case Study Exam Focus
A research team publishes a study claiming that a new mobile health (mHealth) app reduces postpartum depression. The study compared 500 women who used the app with 500 who did not, finding a statistically significant reduction in depression scores. However, women who chose to use the app were more likely to be university-educated and to have private health insurance. The study did not randomly assign the app. (10 points)

(a) Identify the main threat to validity in this study and name the type of bias. (3 points)

(b) The authors argue they "adjusted for education and insurance in a regression model and the effect remained significant." Explain what this means and whether it fully resolves the problem. (4 points)

(c) What study design would you recommend for a definitive evaluation of the app's effectiveness? Justify your choice with at least two reasons. (3 points)

Model Answer

(a) Confounding by indication (selection bias). Women who chose to use the app are systematically different from those who did not — they are more educated, have better insurance, and likely have better health literacy and social support. Any of these factors could independently reduce postpartum depression, regardless of the app. The lack of randomisation means the groups are not exchangeable.

(b) Adjusting means the authors included education and insurance as covariates in a regression model to estimate the app's effect "holding these variables constant." This partially addresses confounding — but it does not fully resolve the problem because: (1) there may be unmeasured confounders (e.g., motivation, social support, prenatal mental health) that differ between groups but were not adjusted for; (2) adjustment assumes the confounders are measured without error, which is rarely true; (3) the groups may differ on characteristics that cannot be quantified. Only randomisation can balance both measured and unmeasured confounders.

(c) Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT) — ideally a pragmatic RCT recruiting pregnant women and randomly assigning them to app vs. standard care. Reasons: (1) Randomisation balances confounders (known and unknown) between groups, providing the strongest evidence for causality. (2) Blinding of outcome assessment reduces information bias. (3) An intention-to-treat analysis preserves the benefits of randomisation. If contamination between app users and controls is a concern, a cluster RCT (randomising clinics or communities) would be an alternative.
