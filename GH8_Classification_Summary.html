GH8: Health System Classification (Seminar 2)

Definitive Guide based on Böhm et al. (2013) & WHO UHC Cube

Group C: Böhm et al. (2013) — 5 Empirical OECD Types

1. What does this scheme classify, and on what criteria?

It classifies health systems based on the dominance of three actors (State, Societal, or Private) across three core dimensions:

TypeRegulationFinancingProvision
1. NHS (National Health Service)StateStateState
2. NHI (National Health Insurance)StateStatePrivate
3. SHI (Social Health Insurance)SocietalSocietalPrivate
4. Etatist SHI (Social Health Insurance State)StateSocietalPrivate
5. PHS (Private Health System)PrivatePrivatePrivate

Example Countries from the paper:

  • NHS: UK (England) Denmark Sweden
  • NHI: Canada Australia New Zealand
  • Etatist SHI: Germany France Japan
  • PHS: USA
2. Where does Germany land — and how confidently?

Germany is classified as Etatist Social Health Insurance (ESHI).

  • Confidence: Very High (1.0 membership).
  • Reasoning: While Financing and Provision remain societal/private, the State now dominates Regulation through legislative control, moving away from the "pure" self-governing SHI model.
3. Apply it to India — where does it land?

India lands in the Private Health System (PHS) category, but in a "fragmented" way.

  • Regulation is officially State-led but weak. In practice, Private actors dominate all three dimensions for the majority of the population who pay out-of-pocket.
4. What can this scheme NOT tell you?

It is a power-based typology. It doesn't tell you:

  • The outcome (how healthy the people are).
  • The efficiency of the spending.

Group D: WHO UHC Cube

1. What does this scheme classify, and on what criteria?

It classifies the coverage gap via three axes:

  • Breadth (Population): WHO is covered?
  • Depth (Services): WHAT is covered?
  • Height (Costs): HOW MUCH is covered by the system (vs. Out-of-pocket)?
3. Apply it to India:

India is a "Thin/Partial Cube".

  • Gap: The "Missing Middle" (informal workers) are left out of Breadth.
  • Problem: Extremely low Height due to high out-of-pocket costs.
