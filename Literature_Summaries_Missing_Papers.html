Literature Summary: Global Health Systems & Performance

Prepared for Seminar 1: Concepts, Frameworks, and Typologies

1. Böhm et al. (2013): Five types of OECD healthcare systems

Main Argument: Traditional labels like "Bismarck" and "Beveridge" are too simplistic. The authors use a deductive approach to classify 30 OECD countries based on who dominates three functions.

The Three Dimensions:

  • Regulation: Who sets the rules? (State, Societal, or Private)
  • Financing: Who pays? (Taxes, Social Insurance, or Private Premiums)
  • Provision: Who delivers care? (Public vs. Private doctors/hospitals)
System Type Regulation Financing Provision Examples
NHS (National Health Service) State State State UK, Sweden
NHI (National Health Insurance) State State Private Canada, Australia
SHI (Social Health Insurance) Societal Societal Private Germany, Austria
Etatist SHI State Societal Private France, Japan
Private Health System Private Private Private USA (pre-ACA)

Hierarchy Rule: The actor who regulates (State) usually determines the financing and provision. Most modern SHI systems are moving toward "Etatist SHI" as governments take more control.

2. Wendt (2014): Changing healthcare system types

Main Argument: Health systems are not static; they evolve through policy reforms. This paper tracks the hybridization of European systems.

  • Institutional Convergence: Countries are increasingly adopting mixed elements. (e.g., NHS systems using private providers, or SHI systems adding tax-based subsidies).
  • New Clusters: Wendt identifies four clusters based on actual performance data:
    1. Health Service Provision-oriented: High focus on delivery.
    2. Universal-Coverage-oriented: Focus on equity and access.
    3. High-Expenditure-oriented: Focus on technology and intensive care.
    4. Low-Expenditure-oriented: Systems constrained by budget.
  • Exam Note: Watch for the shift from "pure" models to "hybrids."

3. Papanicolas & Smith (2013): Performance Comparison [Ch. 1]

Main Argument: Comparing health systems is essential for learning, but we must use a consistent logic to explain why performance differs.

  • The Performance Gap: Countries with the same spending often have very different health outcomes. This gap is usually due to system design (how blocks interact).
  • Functions vs. Goals:
    • Functions: Stewardship, Financing, Resource Generation, Service Delivery.
    • Goals: Health Status, Responsiveness (Dignity/Autonomy), Fair Financing.
  • Role of the State: Even in private systems, the state must act as the Steward (overseer) to prevent market failure.

Seminar Synthesis: These three papers combined show that (1) systems are increasingly regulated by the State, (2) pure historical models are being replaced by hybrids, and (3) performance depends on how well the State "stewards" the interaction between different system blocks.
