1. Böhm et al. (2013): Five Types of OECD Systems

Core Mission: To replace the vague "Bismarck vs. Beveridge" labels with a strict Actor-Function Matrix.

The Three Dimensions of Classification

  • Regulation: Who sets the rules (State vs. Societal vs. Private).
  • Financing: Who pays (Taxes vs. Social Contributions vs. Premiums).
  • Provision: Who delivers (Public hospitals vs. Private/Non-profit).

The 5 Empirical Types (The Table you MUST know)

System Name Regulation Financing Provision Typical Cases
NHS (National Health Service) State State State UK, Sweden, Italy
NHI (National Health Insurance) State State Private Canada, Australia
SHI (Social Health Insurance) Societal Societal Private Germany (Classic), Austria
Etatist SHI State Societal Private France, Japan, Netherlands
Private Health System Private Private Private USA
The "Hierarchy Rule": Böhm argues that Regulation > Financing > Provision. The actor who regulates (the State) usually dictates how the other two function. This explains why "Etatist SHI" is now the most common OECD type.

2. Wendt (2014): Changing Healthcare System Types

Core Mission: To show that systems aren't "frozen"—they are Hybrids in constant reform.

The 4 Performance Clusters

Wendt doesn't just look at *rules*; he looks at *results* (Inpatient index, Outpatient index, gatekeeping). He identifies 4 clusters:

  1. Health Service Provision-oriented: High accessibility and doctor density (e.g., Austria, Germany).
  2. Universal-Coverage-oriented: Maximum equity but often tighter gatekeeping (e.g., UK, Denmark).
  3. High-Expenditure-oriented: Technologically advanced but very expensive (e.g., USA, Switzerland).
  4. Low-Expenditure-oriented: Budget-constrained systems (often Southern Europe).

The Concept of Hybridization

  • Convergence: Systems are becoming more similar (NHS systems using private doctors; SHI systems adding tax money).
  • The "Michelsen Hint": Wendt warns that labels like "Bismarck" are historical ghosts that obscure modern reality.

6. Distinctive Features: The German Case

💡 Why the GKV (GKV = Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) is Unique

  • Origins: Bismarck used it to "buy" worker loyalty (pre-empting Socialism).
  • The Dual System: Coexistence of GKV (Public) and PKV (Private). Typologies often ignore this "internal stratification."
  • Self-Governance (Selbstverwaltung): Historically, the state didn't set prices; the sickness funds and doctors negotiated them directly. Caveat: This is fading as the state takes more control (Etatization).

7. Systems Theory Foundations (GH8 HS FD)

Core Mission: Understand what systems are, how they behave, and why a systems thinking approach matters for health care analysis.

What is a System?

A system is a set of elements that can be distinguished from their environment and are interconnected (networking). The totality of these relationships is not random but rather an order (purpose) that determines the structure of the system.

Types of Systems

TypeDefinitionExample
Open SystemsInteract with their environmentOrganisms, ecological systems, organizations
Closed SystemsDo not exchange matter with the environmentThermos (isolated systems are rare in reality)
Complex SystemsMany interacting parts with unpredictable behaviorHealthcare system, climate, economy

The Macy Conferences (1946–1953) — Birth of Systems Thinking

  • 10 interdisciplinary meetings in New York bringing together biologists, engineers, psychologists, anthropologists.
  • Goal: Develop new ways of thinking about science, society, and the world — moving from things to relations.
  • Foundation of Cybernetics (kybernetes = Greek for "steering"): the transdisciplinary study of circular causal processes — outputs of a system return as inputs (feedback loops).
  • Norbert Wiener: "Control and communication in the animal and the machine."
  • Margaret Mead: "A form of cross-disciplinary thought which made it possible for members of many disciplines to communicate with each other easily in a language which all could understand."
  • Pitts & McCulloch: First mathematical model of a neuron → direct route to AI.
  • Claude Shannon: Introduced entropy, information as measurable, signal vs. noise.
Key Insight: You cannot understand a system by analyzing parts alone. Mind, machine, and society follow the same systemic principles.

Main Schools of Systems Theory

TheoristDisciplineKey Ideas
Bertalanffy (1901–1972) Biology General System Theory: Move from mechanistic, analytical approaches to a holistic perspective. Systems have emergent properties (characteristics appearing only at system level) and self-regulation. Homologies exist across disciplines.
Gregory Bateson (1904–1980) Anthropology, Social Science Eco-system thinking: Individuals are not isolated but parts of interconnected systems. Behavior only makes sense within the whole. Constructivism: individuals act based on their own interpretation of reality. Schismogenesis: recurring behavioral patterns from mutual influence. Communication is about context, tone, and relationship.
Talcott Parsons (1902–1979) Sociology Structural Functionalism: Society is a complex system where different parts (structures) work together to promote order and stability (function). Social Action: human behavior is goal-oriented, meaningful, guided by norms and values. AGIL Framework (see below).

Parsons' AGIL Framework

Every social system must meet four basic needs:

FunctionMeaningKey Question
A — AdaptationHandling the environmentHow does the system secure resources from its environment?
G — Goal AttainmentDeciding and achievingHow does the system define and achieve its objectives?
I — IntegrationKeeping everything coordinatedHow do parts of the system work together harmoniously?
L — Latency (Pattern Maintenance)Maintaining valuesHow does the system preserve and transmit its core values?

Characteristics of Social Systems

  • Self-regulation: Systems regulate themselves in response to environmental changes.
  • Adaptability & Learning ability: Systems evolve and learn from feedback.
  • Self-organization: Order emerges without central control.
  • Constructivism: Reality is our creation — people behave according to their subjective realities.
  • Implicit rules apply in (professional) systems.
  • Everything we do has consequences — even small changes affect the entire system.

Systemic Thinking & Intervention

  • Diagnosis of the social system: What significance do various factors have for a specific situation?
  • Systemic intervention: Based on diagnosis, consider and implement specific actions.
  • Key systemic questions: What are the relationships? Who are the relevant individuals? What explicit and implicit rules exist? Where are the feedback loops and how can they be broken?
  • Interventions trigger counter-reactions (system pushes back — e.g., building roads reduces traffic temporarily, encourages more driving, recreating congestion).

9. Systems Theory: Learning Objectives & Exercises

Learning Objectives: After completing this section you will be able to define what a system is, name its characteristics, identify different types, discuss systems theory and its emergence, explain systems thinking, and provide application examples.

Key Concepts to Master

  • Reductionist approach vs. Network approach — analytic vs. holistic
  • Linearity vs. Cybernetics (circular causality, feedback loops)
  • Emergence: Properties that appear only at system level
  • Self-regulation & Homeostasis
  • System boundaries — what is inside vs. outside
  • Subsystems & Suprasystems

System Dynamics Archetypes (from exercises)

  • Fixes that Fail: Interventions trigger counter-reactions (e.g., building roads → more traffic → congestion returns).
  • Reinforcing Loops: Amplify conflict (arms races, price wars).
  • Success to the Successful: Wealth concentration, elite universities.
  • Limits to Growth: Limited resources, infrastructure bottlenecks.

Important Distinctions

  • Positive Economics: Describes what is (factual, testable statements).
  • Normative Economics: Prescribes what ought to be (value judgments, policy advice).
  • Microeconomics: Individual agents (consumers, households, businesses).
  • Macroeconomics: Economy-wide phenomena (inflation, unemployment, growth).

11. Economics 2.2: Key Terms, Glossary & Economic Methods

Core Mission: Learn the methods of economics and master the foundational terminology.

Economic Models

  • Representations of reality that omit irrelevant variables.
  • Assumption: Ceteris paribus (all other variables remain unchanged).
  • Models make predictions about cause and effect (if… then…).
  • Problem: Other variables are often not constant. Most economic theories cannot be tested experimentally (rely on observations).
  • Association ≠ Causality.

Key Terms

TermDefinitionHealth Care Example
Trade-offVoluntarily sacrificing some of one good/service in exchange for sufficient increase in another.Quality of life vs. treatment options; cost vs. coverage; public health vs. economy
Opportunity CostThe value of the next best alternative forgone. Includes monetary and non-monetary sacrifices (leisure, satisfaction).Hiring skilled staff vs. buying equipment; treating one disease vs. another
UtilityHappiness or satisfaction gained from consuming a good/service. Aggregate utility = social welfare.Patient satisfaction, health outcomes
Marginal AnalysisThe study of consequences of small changes.Cost per additional life-year gained
Marginal UtilityChange in total utility from a one-unit increase in consumption.Diminishing returns of additional treatments
EfficiencyGetting maximum output from given inputs (doing things right).Minimizing waste in hospital operations
EffectivenessAchieving desired outcomes (doing the right things).Does a treatment improve health?

Economic Models in Health Care

  • Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA): Compares costs and health outcomes of interventions, measured in natural units (e.g., cost per life-year gained).
  • Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA): Everything monetized.
  • Cost-Utility Analysis (CUA): Outcomes in QALYs (Quality-Adjusted Life Years) or DALYs.

12. Economics 2.3: Markets & Markets in Health Care

Core Mission: Understand what a market is, how perfect competition works, and why health care markets fundamentally differ.

What is a Market?

A set of arrangements by which buyers and sellers contact each other to exchange goods and services. Markets can be physical or virtual.

Features: Arena, buyers & sellers (supply & demand), one commodity, competition, price.

Perfect Competition (Idealized Model)

ConditionMeaningReality in Health Care
Large number of buyers & sellersNo single actor can influence priceHospitals have local market power
Homogeneous goodsAll producers make identical productsDifferentiated by quality, brand, location
Perfect informationAll relevant variables knownInformation asymmetry (doctor knows more than patient)
No barriers to entryNew firms can enter freelyHigh startup costs, licensing, regulation

Price as a Signal

  • Invisible Hand (Adam Smith): Free markets regulate themselves through prices. Prices summarize global information on supply, demand, and scarcity.
  • Coase (1937): The price mechanism is the defining feature of the market — it coordinates economic activity.
  • When a product is scarce, prices rise → signals producers to make more, consumers to seek alternatives.

Market Types

  • Vary by type of good (wheat), product (smartphones), target group (cosmetic surgery).
  • Broad categories: labor market, financial market, consumer market, healthcare market, black market.
  • Structure: Monopoly (one seller) vs. Perfect Competition (many sellers).

Why Health Care Markets Are Different

  • Information asymmetry: Doctors (suppliers) know more than patients (consumers).
  • Uncertainty: Illness is unpredictable; you cannot plan demand.
  • Externalities: Vaccination benefits others beyond the patient.
  • Equity concerns: Health care is a right, not just a commodity.
  • Third-party payment: Insurance distorts price signals.
  • Barriers to entry: Regulation, licensing, high capital requirements.
  • Not all conditions of perfect competition are met — sometimes none of them are.

13. Economics 2.4: The Role of Government & Decision Making

Core Mission: Understand why the state must play a central role in health systems and how health policy decisions are made.

Why Health Policy?

  • Health is a social goal enshrined in the Constitution (Art. 2, para. 2 in Germany).
  • Health care involves life-and-death decisions, profound uncertainty, and professional asymmetries.
  • Health care is a decisive economic factor and a core issue of social policy.
  • Health inequalities create social tensions.

Definition

Health policy encompasses both the area of social disease prevention and that of social disease management, i.e. the social handling of health risks before and after their occurrence. (Rosenbrock 2014)

Stakeholder Landscape of the German Health System

Cost Bearers / Financing

  • Statutory Health Insurance (GKV)
  • Private Health Insurance (PKV)
  • Long-term care insurance funds
  • Patients & civil society

State & Political Actors

  • Federal Ministry of Health (BMG)
  • State Ministries of Health
  • Bundestag / Health Committees
  • Municipalities & health authorities

Self-Governance

  • Federal Joint Committee (G-BA)
  • Associations of SHI Physicians (KVen)
  • Medical & Nursing Associations

Service Providers

  • Hospitals (public, non-profit, private)
  • Contract doctors & dentists
  • Care facilities, pharmacies, midwives

Special Features of German Health Policy

  • Not determined by government alone — significantly shaped by joint self-government (Selbstverwaltung).
  • Central decisions (financing, benefits catalog of GKV) are made by health insurance companies, doctors, and other stakeholders, not directly by politicians.
  • Operates in an area of tension between economic interests and social responsibility of public services.

Decision Making in Health Care

  • Shaped by cognition, emotion, social influence, available information, and institutional context.
  • Sits at the intersection of cognitive psychology, sociology, economics, and ethics.
  • First and foremost: decision making in health care is about policy.
