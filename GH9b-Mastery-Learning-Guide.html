Module 1: The Basics — Why Quantitative Methods?

▼

💡 The Big Picture

Quantitative research methods help us answer questions like: "How many people have this disease?" "Does this treatment work?" "What is the risk?" Unlike qualitative methods (which explore why and how), quantitative methods give us NUMBERS that we can measure, compare, and analyze statistically.

🌍 The Four Case Scenarios

Throughout this module, you'll learn through REAL global health case scenarios. These are the same cases used in class:

Case 1: Period Poverty in India (Alam et al. 2024)

Research question: What is the prevalence and determinants of period poverty in India?

This study used cross-sectional analysis of the National Family Health Survey-5 data to understand menstruation-related challenges faced by women in India.

Case 2: Long-COVID in HICs vs LMICs

Research question: How does Long-COVID affect different populations?

This examines how the COVID-19 pandemic's long-term effects differ between high-income countries and low/middle-income countries.

Case 3: Diet and Cancer in Spain

Research question: How does diet influence cancer risk?

Examines the relationship between dietary patterns and cancer outcomes in a Spanish cohort.

Case 4: Malaria in Mozambique

Research question: What factors influence malaria transmission and outcomes?

Studies malaria epidemiology in Mozambique, including the impact of climate change on transmission patterns.

🔑 Key Distinction: Prevalence vs. Incidence

Two fundamental measures in epidemiology that students ALWAYS confuse:

FeaturePrevalenceIncidence
What it measuresTotal existing cases at a point in timeNew cases occurring over a time period
NumeratorAll existing casesNew cases only
DenominatorTotal population at that timePopulation at risk (without disease)
Think of it as"How many people HAVE this disease RIGHT NOW?""How many people GOT this disease OVER TIME?"
Study designCross-sectionalCohort, experimental

🍎 Real Example: Diabetes

Prevalence: "In 2024, 10% of adults in Germany have diabetes." (All cases, right now)

Incidence: "In 2024, 5 new cases of diabetes per 1,000 people occur each year." (New cases only, over time)

Prevalence can increase even if incidence stays the same — because people with diabetes live longer!

🧠 Memory Aid
Prevalence = "Picture" (snapshot) • Incidence = "Increase" (new cases over time)

Module 2: Study Designs — The Big Three EXAM FOCUS

▼

🔬 Three Main Observational Study Designs

Every quantitative study fits into one of these designs. The exam WILL test your ability to identify which design was used and what it can/cannot tell us.

The Three Study Designs — Visual Comparison

1️⃣ Cross-Sectional Studies

What? Measure exposure AND outcome at the SAME point in time. Like taking a photograph.

What it CAN do: Measure prevalence, describe characteristics of a population, identify associations

What it CANNOT do: Establish causality (because you don't know which came first — exposure or outcome)

🔄 Standardization — Key Concept for Comparing Studies

💡 Why Standardization Matters

Standardization removes confounding effects when comparing disease frequencies across populations or over time. Without standardization, you risk drawing biased conclusions about disease burden differences.

📊 Pitfalls of Using Crude (Non-Standardized) Measures

  • Crude rates can be misleading when populations differ in age composition (e.g., a country with younger age structure appears to have lower mortality even if underlying risk is higher)
  • Policy decisions based on crude rates may misallocate resources

📈 Types of Standardization (using age as example)

1️⃣ Direct Age Standardization

What it does: Assumes the study population has the same age structure as the standard population.

Formula: Adjusted Rate = Σ (Age-specific rate in study population × Standard population weight)

Use when: You have age-specific rates from your population and want to compare with others.

2️⃣ Indirect Age Standardization

What it does: Applies standard population's age-specific rates to your population's age structure.

Results in: Standardized Mortality Ratio (SMR) or Standardized Incidence Ratio (SIR)

Use when: Age-specific rates for your population are unavailable (common in small studies)

🎯 Choosing the Right Standard Population

  • NESP (New European Standard Population) - Best for European-wide comparisons
  • UN World Standard Population - Best for global or cross-continental work
  • Key rule: Must be the same across all populations/time points being compared

⚖️ Why This Matters for Your Exam

Common Exam Question: A study compares CVD mortality between Country A (young) and Country B (old). Crude rates are 150 vs 210/100,000. After standardization, rates become 350 vs 620/100,000.
Key point: Country B has 77% higher risk after adjustment (620 vs 350), vs only 40% higher with crude rates!
🧠 Remember: Standardization Eliminates the "Age Structure Artifact
Direct = Your rates × Standard weights
Indirect = Standard rates × Your population weights

📋 Example: Period Poverty Study (Alam et al. 2024)

They analyzed the National Family Health Survey-5 in India. They measured period poverty prevalence and associated factors AT ONE POINT IN TIME. This is cross-sectional because they used existing survey data without following people over time.

⚠️ Major Limitation: If you find that period poverty is associated with low income, you CANNOT tell if low income CAUSES period poverty, or if period poverty CAUSES low income, or if a third factor causes both. This is the temporal ambiguity problem.

2️⃣ Case-Control Studies

What? Start with people WHO HAVE the disease (cases) and people WHO DON'T (controls), then look BACK in time to compare past exposures.

The Logic: "People with the disease were exposed more often in the past than people without the disease."

CASES (have disease)
⬅️ Look back in time
Was exposed? Yes/No
CONTROLS (no disease)
⬅️ Look back in time
Was exposed? Yes/No

Measure used: Odds Ratio (OR)

💡 What the OR tells us conceptually

The OR compares the odds of exposure among cases versus controls. It answers: "Were cases more likely to have been exposed than controls?"

  • OR = 1 → No association (cases and controls had same exposure odds)
  • OR > 1 → Cases had higher exposure odds (possible risk factor)
  • OR < 1 → Cases had lower exposure odds (possible protective factor)
⚠️ Key Point: When the disease is RARE, the OR is a good approximation of the Relative Risk (RR). But for common diseases, OR overestimates the RR. This is a common exam trap!

3️⃣ Cohort Studies

What? Start with people BASED ON EXPOSURE STATUS (exposed vs. unexposed), then follow them FORWARD IN TIME to see who develops the disease.

Think of it as: "We'll watch these exposed people and these unexposed people over the next 10 years and see who gets sick."

EXPOSED GROUP
⬇️ Follow forward in time
How many get disease?
UNEXPOSED GROUP
⬇️ Follow forward in time
How many get disease?

Measures used: Relative Risk (RR) and Attributable Risk (AR)

💡 What RR and AR tell us conceptually

Relative Risk (RR) compares the risk in the exposed group to the risk in the unexposed group. It answers: "How many times more (or less) likely is the outcome in the exposed group?"

  • RR = 1 → No difference in risk
  • RR > 1 → Exposure increases risk (e.g., RR = 10 means 10× the risk)
  • RR < 1 → Exposure is protective (e.g., RR = 0.5 means half the risk)

Attributable Risk (AR) is the absolute difference in risk between groups. It answers: "How many excess cases are due to the exposure?"

  • AR = 0 → No excess risk
  • AR > 0 → Some cases are attributable to the exposure
  • AR is measured in the same units as the outcome (e.g., 18 extra cases per 1000 people)

  • Incidence and time-based measures (RR, AR, NNT) require following people forward → only possible in cohort or experimental designs

  • Odds Ratio is the only ratio measure possible in case-control studies (because you select based on disease status, not exposure)
  • OR approximates RR only when the disease is rare — for common diseases, OR overestimates the true risk ratio

Module 3: Effect Measures — Risk, Odds, and More EXAM FOCUS

▼

📏 The Three Main Effect Measures

1️⃣ Relative Risk (RR)

RR: Compares the risk in the exposed group to the risk in the unexposed group. Tells you the strength of an association.

Interpretation:

  • RR = 1.0 → No difference in risk
  • RR = 2.0 → Exposed have DOUBLE the risk
  • RR = 0.5 → Exposed have HALF the risk (protective!)
  • RR = 10 → Exposed have 10× the risk

🍎 Conceptual Example

Smokers have a much higher risk of lung cancer than non-smokers. The RR tells us how many times higher — e.g., smokers might have 10× the risk. RR only tells you about the ratio, not the absolute number of cases.

2️⃣ Odds Ratio (OR)

OR: Compares the odds of an event in one group to the odds in another. Used in case-control studies and logistic regression.

🎲 Odds vs. Probability — Conceptual Difference

Probability = how often something happens out of ALL possible outcomes (e.g., 30 out of 100 people get the disease = 30%)

Odds = how often something happens compared to how often it DOESN'T (e.g., 30 events vs. 70 non-events = 30:70 or roughly 3:7)

Key insight: Odds always look larger than probability. This is why OR > RR for common outcomes — a common exam trap!

3️⃣ Attributable Risk (AR)

Also called Risk Difference — the EXCESS risk in the exposed group due to the exposure.

AR: The absolute difference in risk between the exposed and unexposed groups. Tells you how much disease is due to the exposure in absolute terms.

Interpretation: If AR = 9%, it means 9 out of 100 cases in the exposed group are attributable to the exposure. Unlike RR (which is a ratio), AR gives you the actual public health impact.

4️⃣ Attributable Fraction (AF%)

Attributable Fraction (AF%): The proportion of cases in the exposed group that is caused by the exposure. Tells you the public health impact — how much disease could be prevented if the exposure were removed.

Interpretation: "In smokers, X% of lung cancer cases are due to smoking and would not occur if nobody smoked."

AF% is used when you want to communicate the practical importance of an association, not just its strength.

5️⃣ Number Needed to Treat (NNT)

NNT: The number of people who need to receive an intervention to prevent ONE additional bad outcome (or achieve one additional good outcome).

Conceptual meaning: NNT tells you about clinical efficiency — how many people must be treated for one person to benefit.

  • Small NNT (e.g., 2–5) → highly effective intervention
  • Large NNT (e.g., 100+) → modest benefit, many need treatment for one to benefit
  • Infinite NNT → no effect
  • Negative NNT (NNH) → harm (Number Needed to Harm)

💊 Real Example

A new cholesterol drug has NNT = 50 over 5 years to prevent one heart attack. This means 50 people must take the drug for 5 years to prevent one heart attack — the other 49 get no benefit but may still experience side effects.

🎯 When to Use What?

Quick Guide

  • Prevalence → Cross-sectional studies
  • Incidence & RR → Cohort studies (prospective)
  • Odds Ratio → Case-control studies (or logistic regression)
  • Attributable Risk → When you want to know "how much disease is caused by this exposure"
🧠 POOR PIRATES Use OR
Prevalence = Odds Ratio for RARE diseases

Module 4: Bias, Confounding & Effect Modification EXAM FOCUS

▼

⚠️ Why Studies Can Be Wrong

Three major threats to validity in any study:

ThreatWhat it isHow to control it
Selection Bias Who you include affects the result. Study sample ≠ target population Random sampling, proper recruitment
Information (Measurement) Bias How you measure exposure or outcome affects the result Blinding, validated instruments
Confounding A third variable distorts the exposure-outcome relationship Randomization, matching, stratification, regression

🎭 Confounding — The Classic Trap

Confounder: A variable that is associated with BOTH the exposure AND the outcome, but is NOT on the causal pathway between them.

Three criteria for a confounder:

  1. Must be associated with the EXPOSURE
  2. Must be an independent risk factor for the OUTCOME
  3. Must NOT be on the causal pathway (not an intermediate variable)

Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) — Confounding

🍺 The Classic Example: Coffee and Lung cancer

Studies once showed that coffee drinking was associated with lung cancer. But this was CONFOUNDED by smoking!

  • Coffee drinkers are more likely to SMOKE
  • SMOKING causes lung cancer
  • So smoking confounds the coffee-cancer relationship

The fix: Stratify by smoking status or use multivariable regression to "adjust for" smoking.

🛠️ Strategies for Managing Confounders

Confounders can be handled at two stages of a study. You need to understand when and why each strategy is used:

📐 Design Stage (before data collection)

StrategyHow it worksWhen to use
Randomization Randomly assign exposure — this balances BOTH known and unknown confounders between groups RCTs (gold standard). Only works for experimental studies
Restriction Limit the study to one level of the confounder (e.g., only non-smokers) When the confounder is easy to measure and restrict on; limits generalizability
Matching Select controls so they have the same confounder distribution as cases Case-control studies (frequency or individual matching)

📊 Analysis Stage (after data collection)

StrategyHow it worksWhen to use
Stratification Split data into strata of the confounder and compare effect estimates across strata Simple, intuitive. Limited by number of confounders you can handle at once
Multivariable Regression Include confounders as covariates in the regression model to obtain an "adjusted" effect estimate Most flexible; can handle many confounders simultaneously
Standardization Apply a standard population distribution to remove age/structure differences Comparing rates across populations with different structures (covered in Module 2)

💡 Example: Crude vs. Adjusted Effects

Crude RR (unadjusted): RR = 2.5 — coffee drinkers appear to have 2.5× the risk of lung cancer

Adjusted RR (after controlling for smoking in regression): RR = 1.1 — the effect nearly disappears because smoking was the real cause

The difference between crude and adjusted estimates tells you how much confounding is present. A large change means strong confounding.

⚠️ Residual Confounding: Even after adjustment, some confounding may remain if confounders are measured imprecisely or important confounders are unknown/unmeasured. This is why RCTs (randomization) are the gold standard — they theoretically balance all confounders, even unknown ones.

🔄 Effect Modification (Interaction)

Different from confounding! Effect modification is when the effect of the exposure on the outcome DIFFERS across strata of a third variable.

💊 Example: Drug effect by sex

A drug works well in men but not in women. Sex is an effect modifier — the drug's effect is modified by sex.

Key difference from confounding:

  • Confounding: You want to REMOVE the effect of the confounder
  • Effect modification: You want to REPORT the different effects across strata

🎯 Misclassification Bias

Non-differential misclassification: Error in classification is EQUAL in both groups being compared. Tends to bias TOWARD THE NULL (underestimate the true effect).

Differential misclassification: Error DIFFERS between groups. Can bias in EITHER direction (toward or away from null).

🧠 Recall Bias — The Classic Case

People WITH a disease remember past exposures better than people WITHOUT the disease. This is differential misclassification (cases recall differently from controls) and can strongly bias results.

📊 Types of Variables

Understanding variable types is CRUCIAL for choosing the right statistical test:

TypeDescriptionExamplesVisualization
Categorical (Qualitative) Groups or categories Sex, blood type, yes/no Bar chart
Numerical (Quantitative) Numbers with meaning Age, blood pressure, income Histogram

📏 Measures of Central Tendency & Dispersion

Mean (Average): The sum of all values divided by the count. Use for normally distributed continuous data.

Median (Middle value): The value separating the higher half from the lower half. Use for skewed data, ordinal data, or when outliers are present.

Standard Deviation (SD): Measures how spread out the data is around the mean. ±1 SD covers ~68% of data, ±2 SD covers ~95%, ±3 SD covers ~99.7%.

🎯 Which Measure to Use?

Normal distribution (symmetric, no outliers): Mean ± SD

Skewed distribution or outliers: Median + IQR (Interquartile Range)

Module 6: Confidence Intervals & P-values

▼

📏 Confidence Intervals (CIs)

💡 What is a CI?

A confidence interval gives a RANGE of values that is likely to contain the true population parameter. The 95% CI is most common.

Correct interpretation: "If we repeated this study 100 times, 95 of the resulting CIs would contain the true population parameter."

WRONG interpretation: "There is a 95% probability that the true value lies in this interval." (This is a common exam trap!)

🔍 What CIs Tell Us

  1. Precision: Narrower CI = more precise estimate. Larger sample size → narrower CI.
  2. Statistical significance (for differences):
    • For difference in MEANS: CI excludes 0 → significant
    • For RATIOS (OR, RR, HR): CI excludes 1 → significant
  3. Clinical importance: The range tells us the best-case and worst-case scenarios.

🎲 Real Example: OR with CI

A study reports: OR = 2.5, 95% CI: 1.8 to 3.5

  • The OR is 2.5 (odds are 2.5x higher in exposed group)
  • 95% CI: 1.8 to 3.5 (true OR is likely between 1.8 and 3.5)
  • Since the CI does NOT include 1, the result is statistically significant!

📊 P-values

P-value: The probability of observing the data (or more extreme) IF the null hypothesis is true.

Standard threshold: p < 0.05 (5%) = "statistically significant"

⚠️ Common Misconceptions:
  • ❌ p < 0.05 does NOT mean the null hypothesis is false with 95% probability
  • ❌ p > 0.05 does NOT mean there is NO effect (it just means we can't rule out chance)
  • ❌ Statistical significance ≠ clinical importance
🧠 CI vs P-value
CIs give a RANGE • P-values give a YES/NO answer

📝 Quick Check — Module 6

A study reports RR = 1.5, 95% CI: 1.1 to 2.0. Is this result statistically significant?

Module 7: Regression Basics EXAM FOCUS

▼

📈 Linear Regression

Used when the OUTCOME is continuous (e.g., blood pressure, weight, income).

💡 What Linear Regression Does

Linear regression finds the best straight line through the data points. The slope of that line tells you the effect of the predictor on the outcome.

  • The intercept (β₀) is the predicted outcome when the predictor is zero
  • The slope (β₁) tells you how much the outcome changes for each one-unit increase in the predictor

🍎 Conceptual Example

Study of coffee consumption and blood pressure:

  • The intercept tells you the baseline blood pressure for someone who drinks zero coffee
  • The slope tells you how much blood pressure increases with each additional cup of coffee
  • If the slope is positive → more coffee = higher BP; if negative → more coffee = lower BP

📊 Logistic Regression

Used when the OUTCOME is BINARY (yes/no, disease/no disease).

💡 What Logistic Regression Does

Logistic regression models the probability of an outcome occurring. The output is an Odds Ratio (OR) for each predictor.

  • OR = 1 → predictor has no effect on the outcome
  • OR > 1 → predictor increases the odds of the outcome
  • OR < 1 → predictor decreases the odds (protective)

🔑 Which Regression to Use?

Outcome TypeRegressionOutput
Continuous (BP, weight)Linear regressionβ coefficient (slope)
Binary (disease yes/no)Logistic regressionOdds Ratio (OR)
Count (number of events)Poisson regressionRate Ratio
Time-to-eventCox regressionHazard Ratio (HR)

🔬 Regression & Confounding — The Critical Link

Unadjusted vs. Adjusted Models

Unadjusted (crude) model: Only includes the exposure as a predictor. The result is the raw association — it may be confounded.

Adjusted model: Includes the exposure PLUS potential confounders as additional predictors. The result is the independent effect of the exposure after removing the confounders' influence.

📊 Conceptual Example

Research question: Does coffee consumption affect blood pressure?

  • Crude model: BP = 120 + 2.5 × coffee → each cup increases BP by 2.5 mmHg
  • Adjusted model: BP = 115 + 1.0 × coffee + 5 × age + 3 × smoking
  • After adjusting for age and smoking (confounders), the coffee effect drops from 2.5 to 1.0 — meaning part of the original effect was due to confounding

Interpretation: The adjusted β = 1.0 is the effect of coffee holding age and smoking constant — this is the "independent" effect.

📋 How Regression Controls for Confounders

  • Including a confounder in the regression model estimates the effect of the exposure at each level of the confounder
  • The adjustment answers: "What would the effect be if everyone had the same confounder value?"
  • Comparing crude vs. adjusted estimates tells you how much confounding is present
⚠️ What You Must Know for the Exam: You need to understand conceptually that regression can "adjust for" confounders by including them as additional variables. You should be able to explain why adjustment changes the effect estimate and what "holding other variables constant" means. No formulas needed.

  2. Independent risk factor for the outcome
  3. Not on the causal pathway (not an intermediate)

  1. Design stage: Randomization, restriction, matching
  2. Analysis stage: Stratification, multivariable regression, standardization

Module 9: Cluster RCT Design Variants EXAM FOCUS

▼

🏥 What is a Cluster RCT?

Cluster-randomized controlled trials (cRCTs) allocate groups (e.g., villages, schools, health facilities) rather than individuals to intervention or control conditions. This design is common in Global Health when interventions are delivered at the community or service-delivery level.

Why cRCTs are important in Global Health: Many interventions (water sanitation, community health education, vaccination campaigns) cannot be delivered to individuals in isolation — they naturally affect entire communities. cRCTs are the gold standard for evaluating such interventions.

1️⃣ Parallel (Simple) Cluster RCT

Clusters are randomly assigned once at the start and remain in that arm for the entire follow-up.

AspectDetails
RandomisationCluster (e.g., health centre)
AnalysisAccounts for ICC using mixed-effects models or GEE
✅ AdvantagesStraightforward to implement and analyze; clear separation of intervention vs. control; minimal contamination risk
❌ DisadvantagesRequires a larger number of clusters to achieve statistical power because of ICC; baseline imbalances can occur if few clusters
📌 RelevanceBest choice when interventions are clearly cluster-level and you have enough clusters for adequate power

Example

A malaria-prevention package (bed nets + education) in 20 villages. 10 get the package, 10 standard care. Malaria incidence measured after 12 months.

2️⃣ Stepped-Wedge Cluster RCT

All clusters start in control. At regular intervals, a randomly selected subset crosses over to intervention until all receive it.

AspectDetails
RandomisationCluster, but timing of crossover is randomised
AnalysisTime-adjusted mixed models; accounts for cluster and period effects
✅ AdvantagesEthical appeal: every cluster eventually receives the intervention; allows assessment of temporal trends; often requires fewer clusters than parallel cRCT
❌ DisadvantagesMore complex design and analysis; potential confounding by secular trends (e.g., seasonal disease patterns); longer overall study duration
📌 RelevanceIdeal when it's unethical to withhold the intervention from any cluster, or when the intervention must be rolled out in phases

Example

Ten schools get a nutrition programme. Every 2 months, 2 schools randomly start. By month 10, all have it. BMI measured monthly.

3️⃣ Pair-Matched (Stratified) Cluster RCT

Clusters are paired on key characteristics; within each pair, one is randomised to intervention, the other to control.

AspectDetails
RandomisationCluster, with matching/stratification
AnalysisPaired-difference methods or mixed models with pair-level random effect
✅ AdvantagesImproves balance on important covariates, increasing power; reduces variability from cluster heterogeneity
❌ DisadvantagesRequires reliable baseline data for matching; benefits diminish if matching variables are poorly chosen; more logistical effort in pairing
📌 RelevanceBest when clusters are highly heterogeneous and you have good baseline data for matching

4️⃣ Cluster Crossover RCT

Each cluster receives both intervention and control in different periods with a wash-out phase.

AspectDetails
RandomisationCluster, with crossover order randomised
AnalysisWithin-cluster comparisons using mixed models; must adjust for period and carry-over effects
✅ AdvantagesEach cluster acts as its own control, reducing between-cluster variability; can be efficient with limited clusters
❌ DisadvantagesRisk of carry-over if the intervention has lasting effects; requires sufficient wash-out period (extends study); more complex logistics switching interventions
📌 RelevanceUseful when the number of clusters is small and the intervention effect is short-acting/washes out quickly

5️⃣ Factorial Cluster RCT

Two (or more) interventions evaluated simultaneously (e.g., 2×2 layout). Clusters randomised to: A, B, A+B, or neither.

AspectDetails
RandomisationCluster, factorial allocation
AnalysisMain effects and interaction assessed with mixed models
✅ AdvantagesTests multiple interventions efficiently; can detect synergistic or antagonistic effects (interactions)
❌ DisadvantagesLarger sample size needed to detect interactions; more complex design and interpretation
📌 RelevanceEfficient when you want to test two interventions in the same trial and explore whether they interact

Module 10: ICC & Design Effect

▼

📊 The Problem with Clusters

In cRCTs, individuals within a cluster are likely to be more similar regarding the outcome compared to individuals from different clusters. This similarity reduces the effective sample size, leading to a loss of statistical power.

🔢 Intra-Cluster Correlation Coefficient (ICC)

ICC: Quantifies the similarity of individual responses within clusters. Ranges from 0 (no similarity) to 1 (perfect similarity).

💡 What ICC Means Conceptually

ICC measures how much of the total variability is due to differences between clusters vs. differences within clusters.

  • ICC = 0 → All variability is within clusters; individuals are independent (ideal for standard analysis)
  • ICC = 1 → All variability is between clusters; everyone in a cluster has the same outcome
  • ICC = 0.01 to 0.05 → Typical range in health research; even small ICC reduces power substantially

Typical ICC values: 0.01 to 0.05 in many study scenarios. Always check literature for similar studies or use pilot data.

⚠️ Important: ICC = 1 means all individuals in a cluster have the same outcome — the cluster effectively contributes only 1 data point. ICC = 0 means no clustering effect — effective sample size equals total individuals.

📐 Design Effect (DE)

Design Effect: The factor by which you must multiply the sample size of an individually randomized trial to achieve the same power in a cRCT.

💡 What DE Tells You

The Design Effect tells you how much larger your sample needs to be because of clustering.

  • DE = 1 → No clustering effect (ICC = 0); no adjustment needed
  • DE = 2 → You need twice as many participants as an individually randomized trial
  • DE depends on: cluster size (larger clusters → bigger DE) and ICC (higher ICC → bigger DE)

🧮 Effective Sample Size (ESS)

Effective Sample Size: The number of independent observations your study actually has after accounting for clustering. Always less than the total number of participants.

💡 Why ESS Matters

If you have 300 participants but ESS = 190, you only have 190 "worth" of independent data. Your study has the statistical power of a 190-person individually randomized trial, not 300. This is why cRCTs need more participants than individual RCTs.

💡 Takeaway

Always account for clustering when designing and analyzing cRCTs. The ICC reduces your effective sample size, and the Design Effect tells you how much larger your sample needs to be.

📝 Quick Check — Module 10

A cRCT has 20 clusters with 25 individuals each. ICC = 0.05. What does this mean for the study's power?

The study has more power than an individual RCT
The effective sample size is reduced, so the study needs more participants to maintain power

✓ Correct! Any ICC > 0 reduces the effective sample size due to clustering. The Design Effect (DE) tells you how much larger the sample needs to be to compensate. In this example, DE = 1 + (25−1) × 0.05 = 2.2 — meaning the study needs 2.2× the sample size of an individual RCT.

Module 11: Time-to-Event (Survival) Analysis EXAM FOCUS

▼

⏱️ What is Time-to-Event Data?

When the time until an event happens is more important than whether the event happens at all, we use survival analysis. Examples: time until death, disease remission, or recurrence.

📌 Key Concepts

Survival time: The time span from a well-defined starting point (e.g., diagnosis, study baseline) until the occurrence of a well-defined event (death, remission, recurrence, or end of observation).

Right-censoring: When an individual does not have the event during the observation period — either because they left the study, the study ended, or they were lost to follow-up. Censored observations are still informative!

📈 Kaplan-Meier Estimator

The Kaplan-Meier estimator (KME) estimates the survival function S(t) = P(T > t) — the probability of surviving beyond time t.

💡 How the KM Estimator Works (Conceptually)

The KM estimator calculates the probability of surviving up to each point in time by multiplying the probabilities of surviving each earlier time point.

  • At each time point where an event occurs, the survival probability drops
  • When a patient is censored, they are removed from the "risk set" but the survival probability does NOT drop
  • The result is a step function — flat between events, dropping only when events occur

📋 Conceptual Example

In a study of 31 patients with severe liver disease:

  • At the start, everyone is alive → survival = 100%
  • When the first patient dies, survival drops slightly
  • When a patient is censored (leaves the study or reaches the end), survival stays the same — but that patient no longer contributes to future calculations
  • Key insight: Censored observations reduce the risk set but do NOT cause a drop in the survival function

📊 The Kaplan-Meier Curve

Kaplan-Meier Survival Curves — Treatment vs. Control

How to Read a KM Curve

  • The curve is a step function — it drops only when events occur
  • Censored observations are marked with tick marks (no drop)
  • Median survival = time when S(t) ≤ 0.5 (dashed line intersection)
  • Wider steps = fewer people at risk (less precision)

🧪 Log-Rank Test

Used to test whether two (or more) survival functions are statistically different.

Null hypothesis: The survival functions are the same: S(t)1 = S(t)2

The log-rank test is a χ²-like test procedure that compares observed vs. expected events across time points.

🧠 Survival Analysis in a Nutshell
KM = Step function • Censoring = No drop • Log-rank = Compare two curves • Median = 50% survival time

📝 Quick Check — Module 11

What happens to the Kaplan-Meier survival estimate when a patient is censored at time t?

✓ Correct! Censoring does not cause a drop in the survival function. The censored individual is included in the risk set up until their censoring time, then removed from subsequent calculations.

Module 12: Sample Size Planning EXAM FOCUS

▼
📌 PROF. SIEBERT SAYS: Sample size planning is VERY IMPORTANT for the exam. You need to understand why it matters and how it's approached conceptually — not memorize formulas.

❓ Why Is Sample Size Planning Important?

Sample size planning answers a fundamental question: "How many participants do I need for my study to be meaningful?"

  • Too small → Study may fail to detect a real effect (false negative), wasting time and resources
  • Too large → Waste of resources, may detect trivial effects that are statistically significant but clinically meaningless
  • Ethical issue: Too many or too few participants exposes people to research without sufficient scientific benefit
  • Funding: Grant reviewers expect a clear justification of the sample size

🔑 Key Concept: Statistical Power

Statistical Power: The probability of detecting a true effect if it exists. Usually set at 80% (meaning if there is a real effect, you'll find it 8 out of 10 times).

Power depends on four things (conceptually):

FactorEffect on Sample Size
Larger effect size (bigger difference between groups)→ Need fewer participants (easier to detect)
Higher variability (more spread in the data)→ Need more participants (harder to detect signal)
Higher desired power (e.g., 90% vs. 80%)→ Need more participants
Stricter significance level (α = 0.01 vs. 0.05)→ Need more participants

📋 How Sample Size Planning Is Done (Conceptual Steps)

  1. Define the research question — What comparison are you making?
  2. Choose the study design — Cross-sectional, cohort, case-control, RCT, cRCT
  3. Specify the primary outcome — What will you measure?
  4. Estimate the expected effect size — From literature, pilot studies, or clinically meaningful difference
  5. Set power and significance level — Usually 80% power, α = 0.05
  6. Account for design features — For cRCTs, multiply by the Design Effect (DE) to account for clustering
  7. Add attrition buffer — Increase by 10-20% to account for dropouts/loss to follow-up

🎯 Special Considerations by Study Design

DesignSample Size Consideration
Cross-sectionalBased on expected prevalence and desired precision of the estimate
Case-controlDepends on expected exposure prevalence in controls and the OR you want to detect
CohortDepends on expected incidence in unexposed and the RR you want to detect
cRCTMust calculate sample size for an individual RCT, then multiply by Design Effect (DE) — this accounts for clustering

💡 Key Takeaways for the Exam

  • Sample size is about balancing scientific rigor with practical constraints
  • Power = 80% is the standard — there's a 20% chance of missing a true effect
  • Larger effects need fewer participants; smaller effects need more
  • For cRCTs, the Design Effect (DE) increases the required sample size
  • Sample size calculations should always be reported and justified in research proposals

🧠 Flashcards — Surati Paper + Course Concepts

Card 1

What study design did Surati et al. use, and which effect measure is appropriate?
📖 Click to reveal

Cross-sectional study. They measured exposure (social support, breastfeeding attitudes, self-efficacy) and outcome (PPD) at the same point in time. The appropriate effect measure is Prevalence OR from logistic regression. They cannot calculate RR, incidence, or AR because there is no follow-up over time.

Card 2

Why can't Surati et al. claim causality between low social support and PPD?
📖 Click to reveal

Temporal ambiguity. In a cross-sectional study, you don't know which came first — did low social support cause PPD, or did PPD cause women to perceive less social support? Both directions are plausible. This is the fundamental limitation of cross-sectional designs.

Card 3

The paper reports AOR = 2.51 for low social support (95% CI: 1.57–4.02). Interpret this.
📖 Click to reveal

Women with low social support have 2.51 times the odds of PPD compared to women with high social support, after adjusting for confounders (education, SES, breastfeeding status, depression history, etc.). The 95% CI (1.57–4.02) excludes 1, so this is statistically significant. The wide CI suggests some imprecision — the true effect could be anywhere from 1.57× to 4.02×.

Card 4

The paper used multivariate logistic regression. Why was this necessary instead of just reporting crude ORs?
📖 Click to reveal

To control for confounding. Crude ORs may be biased by confounders — e.g., SES is associated with both social support (lower SES → less support) and PPD (lower SES → more depression). By including confounders as covariates in the regression model, the adjusted OR represents the independent effect of each variable holding others constant. This is exactly what Module 7 teaches about regression with confounders.

Card 5

PPD prevalence was 50.1%. What does this imply about using OR to approximate RR?
📖 Click to reveal

Since PPD is not rare (50.1% prevalence), the OR cannot approximate the RR. For common outcomes, OR overestimates the true risk ratio. The authors correctly report ORs. If they had reported RRs, they would need a different study design (cohort) and a different regression model (log-binomial or Poisson). This is a classic exam trap — always check disease prevalence before interpreting OR as RR!

Card 6

Name two confounders the authors adjusted for and explain why each meets the three criteria for a confounder.
📖 Click to reveal

Example 1 — Socioeconomic Status (SES): (1) Associated with exposure — lower SES women have less social support; (2) Independent risk factor for PPD — lower SES increases stress; (3) Not on causal pathway — SES is not caused by social support or PPD.

Example 2 — Education: (1) Lower education is associated with lower breastfeeding self-efficacy; (2) Lower education is a risk factor for PPD; (3) Education precedes both exposure and outcome, not on causal pathway.

Card 7

The correlation heatmap shows r = −0.47 for PPD and self-efficacy. Interpret this.
📖 Click to reveal

There is a moderate negative correlation between PPD and self-efficacy. As self-efficacy increases, PPD tends to decrease (and vice versa). The correlation coefficient −0.47 means it's a moderate-strength linear relationship. However, correlation ≠ causation — this could be bidirectional, or a third factor (like prior depression) could influence both. This aligns with Module 5's teaching on correlation.

Card 8

How was the sample size of 403 calculated, and what design feature increased the required sample?
📖 Click to reveal

Sample size was calculated using the formula for a single proportion: 95% CI, 5% margin of error, 31% anticipated prevalence (from prior studies), plus 20% for non-response. The two-stage cluster sampling (PHCs selected by PPS, then women within PHCs) introduces a Design Effect due to clustering — women within the same PHC are more similar. A cluster-adjusted sample size would multiply by DE = 1 + (m−1) × ICC. The paper does not explicitly state the DE adjustment, which is a common limitation.

🎯 Exam-Style Questions on Surati et al.

Question 1 (Likely Exam Format)

A researcher wants to replicate Surati et al.'s study but using a cohort design instead of cross-sectional. Describe how the design would differ, what new measures become possible, and what additional resources would be needed.
📖 Click to see model answer

Design change: Instead of measuring social support and PPD at one time, the researcher would recruit women before or immediately after childbirth, measure social support/self-efficacy at baseline, then follow them forward for 6–12 months to track who develops PPD.

New measures possible: Cumulative incidence, Relative Risk (RR), Attributable Risk (AR), Attributable Fraction (AF%), NNT — none of which are possible in a cross-sectional study. Kaplan-Meier curves could estimate time-to-PPD.

Additional resources: Longer study duration (follow-up over time), more staff for repeated assessments, strategies to minimise loss to follow-up, higher cost.

Question 2 (Likely Exam Format)

In Surati et al., the crude OR for low social support was 3.28 (95% CI: 2.14–5.03). After adjustment, AOR = 2.51 (95% CI: 1.57–4.02). What does this change tell you about confounding in this association?
📖 Click to see model answer

The OR decreased from 3.28 to 2.51 after adjustment — a reduction of about 23%. This means part of the crude association was due to confounding by other variables (education, SES, depression history, etc.). The adjusted OR (2.51) is the independent effect of low social support, holding confounders constant. However, the association remains significant after adjustment (CI still excludes 1), indicating a robust independent effect. In the exam, you would explain that the difference between crude and adjusted estimates quantifies the amount of confounding present.

Question 3 (Likely Exam Format)

The study found that non-exclusive breastfeeding was associated with higher odds of PPD (AOR = 1.68, p = 0.014). Explain two possible causal mechanisms for this association, and why the cross-sectional design cannot distinguish between them.
📖 Click to see model answer

Mechanism 1 (Breastfeeding difficulties → PPD): Women who struggle with breastfeeding experience stress, frustration, and sleep disruption, which triggers or worsens depressive symptoms.

Mechanism 2 (PPD → Breastfeeding difficulties): Women with PPD have low motivation, energy, and confidence, making it harder to maintain exclusive breastfeeding, leading to early cessation or supplementation.

Why cross-sectional can't distinguish: Both mechanisms are plausible and both predict the same cross-sectional association. Without knowing the temporal sequence (which came first), we cannot determine the causal direction. This is the temporal ambiguity problem — the fatal flaw of cross-sectional designs for causal inference. A cohort study with repeated measures would be needed.
